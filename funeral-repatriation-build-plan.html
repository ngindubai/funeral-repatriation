1. Business Overview

The Opportunity

When a person dies in a foreign country, the family needs to bring the body home. This is 100% cross-border, always urgent, always paid upfront, and there is no global SEO player. The information asymmetry is total: nobody researches funeral repatriation before they need it.

Scored 8.5/10 in the cross-border opportunity report. Ranked #4 overall. #1 in competition vacuum (no global player exists). #1 information asymmetry (families have zero visibility into local costs or processes).

Market Size

Estimated 25,000-50,000 international repatriation cases per year from English-speaking countries alone. Growing with increased international travel and expatriate populations.

Average Deal Value

$5,000-$20,000 per repatriation case. Higher for remote countries (Africa, Asia) due to logistics complexity. Margin: 40-60% after local funeral director, airline cargo, and documentation costs.

Competition

Virtually zero at global scale. A handful of small UK-based repatriation firms. No programmatic SEO player. No site covering "repatriation from [every country]". Wide open.

2.3 Content Requirements: Sensitivity Is Everything

Critical: Tone and Ethics

This is not bus hire. Every visitor to this site is in crisis. A loved one has died. The content must be:

  • Empathetic, never commercial. No "Book now!" or "Get a quote in 60 seconds!" CTAs. Instead: "We're here to help. Call us any time." and "Let us take care of the arrangements."
  • Factually accurate. Legal requirements for repatriation vary by country. Wrong information could cause a family to miss a deadline or fail to obtain the right documents. Every country page must be researched and verified.
  • Transparent about costs. Families in crisis are vulnerable. No hidden fees. No upselling. Clear price ranges upfront. This builds trust and differentiates from less scrupulous operators.
  • Never exploitative. No fear-based marketing. No urgency pressure. No "limited time" offers (seriously). The urgency is real enough without manufacturing it.
  • Culturally aware. Different cultures have different practices. Islamic burial requires interment within 24 hours. Hindu traditions prefer cremation. Jewish law requires prompt burial. Buddhist customs vary by country. Content must acknowledge and respect this.

Tone Rules (Beyond Humaniser)

  • Replace "purchase" / "buy" / "order" with "arrange" / "organise"
  • Replace "customer" / "client" with "family" / "you"
  • Replace "service" with "support" or "assistance" where possible
  • Replace "price" / "cost" / "fee" with "arrangement costs" or "typical costs"
  • Never use exclamation marks
  • Never use urgency language ("Act now", "Don't delay")

14. SEO & LLM Optimisation: Parallel Plan

Traditional SEO and LLM citation optimisation run in parallel from Phase 2 onwards. This is a YMYL site (death, legal, financial). Google and LLMs hold this content to the highest E-E-A-T standards. Every optimisation decision must balance discoverability with sensitivity and accuracy.

Traditional SEO Track

Title Formula (Sensitivity-First)

Pattern: [Service] from [Country] | [Supportive Phrase] | [Brand]

Example: "Repatriation from Thailand | Bringing Your Loved One Home | [Brand]"

Never use urgency language, commercial pressure, or pricing in titles. "Helping families" not "cheap repatriation". "Step-by-step guide" not "fast service".

Schema Markup (Per Page Type)

  • Country pages: FAQPage + Service + BreadcrumbList. Cost ranges in structured data where verified.
  • City pages: LocalBusiness (funeral directors) + FAQPage + BreadcrumbList
  • Guide pages: HowTo + FAQPage + Article (author attribution mandatory)
  • Blog posts: Article + FAQPage + author Person schema with credentials

Internal Linking Architecture

  • Country page links to: city pages, guide page, cremation page, ashes page, embassy page
  • Guide pages link to: all relevant country pages (contextual)
  • Blog posts link to: 2-3 country pages, 1 guide page (natural placement)
  • Hub pages per silo: /repatriation/, /cremation-transfer/, /ashes-transport/
  • Cross-silo links only where genuinely helpful to the reader
LLM Citation Track

Why LLMs Matter for Repatriation

Families in crisis Google "what do I do when someone dies abroad" and increasingly ask ChatGPT, Perplexity, or Gemini. If our content is cited as the answer, we become the first point of contact. LLM citation is not a replacement for SEO; it is a parallel channel that compounds with rankings.

llms.txt Strategy

Create /llms.txt and /llms-full.txt at site root. Structure:

  • Site identity: who we are, what we do, which countries we cover
  • Service summary: repatriation, cremation transfer, ashes transport
  • Country coverage count and P1 list
  • Key data points: average cost ranges, typical timelines, required documents
  • Update cadence: refreshed monthly with new country data

Entity-First Content for Citation

Structure every country page to answer the core questions directly:

  • "How long does repatriation from [country] take?" (direct answer first, then detail)
  • "How much does it cost to bring a body home from [country]?" (range with source)
  • "What documents do I need?" (numbered list, not prose)
  • "Who do I contact first?" (embassy details, then local contacts)

Each answer block becomes an extraction target for LLMs. Tables with columns (Document | Required By | Where to Get | Typical Wait) are ideal.

Compassionate Query Targeting

LLM queries for this topic are emotionally loaded. Match tone:

  • "My father died in Spain, what do I do" (process guide, not sales pitch)
  • "Can I bring ashes on a plane from Thailand" (yes/no answer, then airline rules)
  • "How to bring a body home from abroad" (step-by-step, empathetic, practical)
  • "Repatriation insurance, is it worth it" (honest assessment, not upsell)

Backlink Strategy (Sensitivity-Appropriate)

Authority Sources
  • NAFD (National Association of Funeral Directors)
  • SAIF (Society of Allied and Independent Funeral Directors)
  • FIAT-IFTA (International Federation of Thanatologists)
  • GOV.UK "when someone dies abroad" page (citation, not link)
  • FCO/FCDO travel advisory pages
Community & Support
  • Expat forums (ExpatForum, InterNations, Expat.com)
  • Bereavement support charities (Cruse, WAY Foundation)
  • Travel safety bloggers and insurance comparison sites
  • Embassy resource pages
  • Religious and cultural community organisations
Content Partnerships
  • Guest articles for funeral industry trade publications
  • Expert quotes for travel insurance comparison articles
  • Resource guides for consular assistance pages
  • Academic citations: mortality research, expat demographics
  • Charity partnerships: sponsored guides, free helpline listings

YMYL Compliance Checklist

Non-Negotiable for Every Page
  • Author attribution with verifiable credentials (funeral industry experience, repatriation expertise)
  • Named, dated sources for all legal requirements and cost figures
  • "Last verified" date on every country page (embassy contacts change frequently)
  • No implied guarantees: "typically takes 5-10 working days" not "we guarantee 5-day turnaround"
  • Medical/legal disclaimers where relevant (post-mortem requirements, death certificates)
  • Clear distinction between information and advice ("consult your local funeral director" not "you should do X")
  • Privacy policy covering handling of sensitive personal data (bereavement enquiries)
