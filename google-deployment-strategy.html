3. The Real Risks — Not What You Think

The question is not "will Google penalise us for publishing too many pages?" The correct question is "will Google trust the pages we publish?" For a YMYL site on a new domain, here are the actual risks, ranked.

⚠
CRITICAL — Scaled Content Abuse Detection
Google's 2024/2025 spam policy updates explicitly target "scaled content abuse" — pages generated at scale that provide little to no value to users. Google's John Mueller has publicly stated "Programmatic SEO is often a fancy banner for spam." The test is not page count; it's whether each page genuinely helps the user in a way no other page on the site does. City pages and embassy pages are the highest-risk silos. Country hubs and guides are the safest.
⚠
CRITICAL — New Domain Trust (Zero E-E-A-T Signals)
repatriateservice.com is a new domain with zero backlinks, zero domain authority, and zero historical trust. For YMYL content (death, legal, financial), Google applies the highest E-E-A-T scrutiny. A new domain with 269 pages on a YMYL topic will rank poorly until it has earned trust through backlinks, author attribution, cited sources, and user engagement signals. This is the primary ranking constraint — not crawl budget.
△
MEDIUM — Doorway Abuse (Thin City Pages)
Google's spam policy defines doorway abuse as "having multiple domain names or pages targeted at specific regions or cities that funnel users to one page." City pages that contain minimal unique information beyond what the country hub already says are at risk of being filtered as doorway pages. Each city page must contain genuine, unique information specific to that city.
△
MEDIUM — Low Initial Crawl Demand (Not a Penalty — Expected Behaviour)
New domains naturally start with low crawl demand because Google has no popularity or freshness signals yet. Pages will be discovered slowly. This is not a penalty — it is the natural ramp-up for a new domain. Crawl demand grows as the site earns backlinks, generates user engagement, and demonstrates page quality. Submitting a sitemap to Google Search Console accelerates this.
✓
LOW — Deploying All 269 Pages at Once
There is no Google mechanism that penalises "dumping" a large number of pages at launch. Google has explicitly stated crawl budget management is only for 1M+ page sites. Deploying all current pages in a single go is not a risk. Google will simply crawl and index them at whatever pace its crawl demand calculates for a new domain. There is no benefit to artificially throttling deployment.

"Programmatic SEO is often a fancy banner for spam."

John Mueller, Google Search Advocate — July 2023. The key distinction: does each page provide unique, genuine value to the user?

4. Should You Split Deployment? — The Direct Answer

Splitting Deployment Does NOT Help With:
  • Avoiding a Google "large site penalty" — this does not exist for sites under 1M pages
  • Crawl budget — irrelevant at this scale
  • Indexing speed — pages get indexed based on quality and authority, not arrival timing
  • Domain authority — authority is built through backlinks, not staged page releases
  • Avoiding spam detection — thin content is thin regardless of when it is published
What DOES Help With Indexing Success:
  • Verifying the domain in Google Search Console before deployment
  • Submitting sitemap immediately after going live
  • Strong internal linking so Googlebot discovers all pages through links, not just sitemap
  • Each page having unique, factual, well-cited content
  • Author attribution and trust signals on every page
  • Building backlinks from relevant, authoritative sources post-launch
  • Excluding genuinely thin pages until content is improved
  • Fast page load times (Hugo static site — strong advantage here)
Recommendation: Deploy in full when ready. Do not artificially split. The only valid reason to stage deployment is content quality — not page count or timing. If certain silos (e.g. embassy contact pages with minimal editorial content) are thin, exclude them from the sitemap using _build: { list: never } frontmatter or robots.txt, and add them only once content meets quality thresholds. Deploy everything else at launch.

6. Quality Gate — Protecting Against Scaled Content Abuse Detection

Google's scaled content abuse policy applies when pages are generated "for the primary purpose of manipulating search rankings and not helping users." The distinction between acceptable programmatic content and spam comes down to whether each page has genuine, unique value. Use this checklist to evaluate every silo before deployment.

Safe Silos — High Unique Value Per Page
  • Country hubs — Each covers a distinct jurisdiction, cost structure, documentation regime, and timeline. Unique data per country from authoritative sources.
  • Guides — Long-form, expert-authored, heavily cited. Clear E-E-A-T signals.
  • Blog — Informational, time-indexed, not duplicative of country hubs.
  • FAQ pages — Answer-specific, schema-marked, non-duplicative of each other.
  • Bringing ashes home — Distinct process from repatriation of a body. Unique content needed per country.
Silos Requiring Quality Review Before Deployment
  • City pages — Highest risk. Must have unique local information (local funeral directors, airport details, local legal requirements) per city. Pages that only repeat country hub information are doorway pages.
  • Embassy contact pages — Data-driven, low editorial depth. Acceptable only if they include actionable process information alongside contact data, not just address listings.
  • Cremation transfer pages — Must demonstrate unique process/documentation differences between countries, not just reformatted country hub content.
Per-Page Quality Checklist (Run Before Enabling Any Page in Sitemap)
  • Does this page answer a search query that is distinct from every other page on the site?
  • Does this page contain at least one piece of information a user cannot find on the parent country hub?
  • Is there at least one named, dated external source cited for a factual claim?
  • Is the page clearly attributable to an expert author or institutional source?
  • Does the page have more than 300 words of unique body content (not frontmatter)?
  • Are there at least 2 internal links with descriptive anchor text?
  • Is there a clear call to action that serves the user's immediate need?
  • City page only: Does this page contain at minimum the local airport IATA code, one named local funeral director or consular contact, and one city-specific logistical note?
  • Embassy page only: Does this page include the correct current consular procedure, not just address data?

8. Sitemap Management — Practical Guidance

Current State

Hugo auto-generates /sitemap.xml at build time. It includes all pages not excluded by frontmatter. At 269 pages, the site is at 0.5% of the 50,000-URL sitemap limit. No sitemap index file needed.

What Google Uses From Your Sitemap

Google reads: <loc> (URL) and <lastmod> (last modified date, if consistently accurate). Google ignores: <priority> and <changefreq>. Hugo's sitemap includes lastmod from file modification date — keep this accurate.

Excluding Thin Pages From the Sitemap (Without Deleting Them)

To keep a page on the site but exclude it from the sitemap and Google's index, add to frontmatter:

_build:
  list: never
  render: always
sitemap:
  disable: true

This keeps the page accessible via direct link but removes it from sitemap discovery. Use for any page that fails the quality checklist until it is improved. Do NOT use robots.txt noindex — Google wastes crawl budget on noindexed pages.

9. When Should You Stop Building New Pages?

Short answer: Stop building when content quality per page cannot be maintained — not before. There is no Google-imposed limit on how many pages you can build. The decision to stop building should be driven entirely by your ability to produce genuinely useful, well-sourced, unique content per page. If the data and editorial capacity exists, build more pages. If it does not, stop.

[CRITICAL 1] Placeholder phone number on every page — site cannot go live

BLOCKER

Evidence from built HTML (site/public/repatriation-from-spain/index.html, lines 1-2):

BLOCKER
