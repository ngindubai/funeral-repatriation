Competitor Analysis: Funeral Repatriation SEO

Chunk 0D | Researched 15 April 2026 | Data sources: live site scraping (Rowland Brothers International), GOV.UK death abroad guidance, industry knowledge

Executive Summary

The funeral repatriation market online is dominated by Rowland Brothers International (est. 1873, repatriation since 1971). They have 150+ country pages, but every single one is thin: one sentence, a starting price, and a contact form. No process guides, no document checklists, no embassy contacts, no cultural guidance, no FAQs. This is the single biggest SEO opportunity in this vertical. Their brand authority is strong. Their content is weak. We beat them with depth, not with history.

Three other planned competitors (Kenyon International, Repatriation UK, DFS Memorials) either operate in a different market segment (Kenyon: disaster response/B2B) or were not accessible for analysis. The competitive landscape is narrow. A content-led SEO entrant with 800-1,200 word country pages, guide content, and proper schema markup can outrank pages that contain 30 words of unique content.

Country Page Analysis (Critical Finding)

MAJOR OPPORTUNITY: Country pages are extremely thin content

Every single country page on Rowland Brothers follows the same template. The unique content per page is approximately 30-40 words. The rest is a shared contact form and a list of other countries in the region. That's it. No process guide, no documents checklist, no embassy contacts, no timeline, no cost breakdown, no cultural practices, no FAQ.

Typical Country Page Structure (e.g. /repatriation-to-spain/)

  1. Hero image: Generic stock photo (same across multiple countries)
  2. H1: "Repatriation to Spain"
  3. Body content (entire unique text): "Our complete repatriation service from UK to Spain starts from GBP 1,900. Rowland Brothers International offers families a complete repatriation service from the UK to Spain, Spain to UK, from any country to Spain. Our experienced team is committed to working with you to deliver a smooth, professional and stress-free repatriation service."
  4. Contact Us CTA button
  5. Sidebar/below: List of all other countries in the same region (Europe) with links
  6. Contact form: Name, email, phone, message, Send button
  7. Footer: Standard site footer

Word count of unique content: approximately 50 words.

No FAQPage schema. No FuneralHome schema. No BreadcrumbList visible. No structured data found.

What They Do Well

  • Brand authority: 55 years in international repatriation. Genuine pioneer status.
  • Coverage: 150+ country pages. Covers more countries than any competitor.
  • Multi-audience: Serves families, funeral directors, corporates, assistance companies, diplomats. Multiple revenue streams.
  • Accreditations: ISO 9001, FIAT-IFTA, Armed Forces Covenant. Trust signals are strong.
  • Pricing transparency: They show starting prices on country pages and the "Death in UK" page. Unusual in this industry.
  • Active blog: Recent content (March 2026) on relevant topics. Shows Google the site is maintained.
  • Freephone number: 0800 number is a strong trust signal for UK families.
  • Bereavement support: Foundation for bereavement, education days. Shows genuine care beyond commercial interest.
  • Multi-denomination: Experience with all religious denominations. Ritual washing, specific ceremonies.
  • Coffin/casket sales: Separate e-commerce channel (rowlandbrothersshop.com). Additional revenue.

Where They Are Weak

  • Country pages are thin: 50 words of unique content per page. Google's Helpful Content Update penalises this. An 800+ word page with genuine guidance will outperform.
  • No process information: A grieving family visiting their Spain page learns nothing about the actual repatriation process from Spain. No steps, no documents, no timeline.
  • No embassy contacts: No mention of British Embassy or consulate in any country page. A family searching "what to do if someone dies in Spain" gets no practical help.
  • No FAQ content: Zero FAQ sections. No FAQPage schema. Misses "People Also Ask" entirely.
  • No structured data: No FuneralHome schema, no FAQPage, no visible BreadcrumbList JSON-LD. Misses rich results.
  • No guide/informational content: No "What to do if someone dies in [Country]" guide pages. These are the highest-volume informational queries.
  • No cremation transfer / ashes transport separation: Everything is under "repatriation." No separate content targeting "cremation transfer" or "shipping ashes from" queries.
  • No cultural/religious guidance: Despite serving all denominations, country pages contain zero cultural information. Families must call to find out.
  • Diluted focus: B2B pages (funeral directors, assistance companies, corporates, diplomats) sit alongside family-facing content. Navigation is split. Not clear if the site is for families or for industry professionals.
  • No cost breakdowns: Just "starting from GBP X,XXX." No explanation of what drives cost, what's included, what's additional.
  • Same stock imagery: Multiple country pages appear to use the same generic images.
  • WordPress performance: WordPress with multiple plugins. Likely slower than a static site build.

Our strategy of deep content + proper EEAT signals addresses both.

4. Government Guidance (GOV.UK)

GOV.UK: "What to do if someone dies abroad"

URL: gov.uk/after-a-death/death-abroad

This is the top-ranking informational page for many "death abroad" queries. It outranks all commercial providers for informational intent. Key content:

Bringing the body home (from GOV.UK)

  1. Register the death with local authorities in the country where the person died
  2. Get a certified English translation of the death certificate
  3. Get permission to remove the body, issued by a coroner (or equivalent) in the country of death
  4. Tell a coroner in England or Wales if the cause of death was unknown, violent or unnatural
  5. Country-specific bereavement packs available at gov.uk
  6. Once home: take death certificate to register office, get "certificate of no liability to register"

Bringing ashes home (from GOV.UK)

  • Need: death certificate + certificate of cremation
  • Each country has its own rules about departing with human ashes
  • Contact airline about hand luggage vs checked luggage for ashes
  • Non-metallic container may be required for x-ray
  • Do not cremate abroad if you want a UK coroner to conduct an inquest

Tell Us Once Service

GOV.UK's "Tell Us Once" service lets families report a death to most government organisations in one go. Available if the person normally lived in England, Scotland or Wales and was abroad temporarily.

NOTE: GOV.UK content is Open Government Licence v3.0

We cannot copy GOV.UK text, but we can (and should) link to it as an authoritative source. Our guide pages should complement GOV.UK by adding: country-specific detail GOV.UK doesn't cover, embassy contact details, practical next-step guidance, emotional support signposting, and links to our repatriation service. We are "the human help that fills the gap between the government guidance and getting your loved one home."

  • Our content strategy should include cost guidance sections that explain what drives the price (distance, local regulations, time of year, religious requirements) without quoting specific numbers until the business model is set

8. Strategic Implications for Our Build

Do Different

  • Deep content, not wide. RBI has 150 thin pages. We start with 26 countries but each page has 800-1,200 words of genuine, helpful, country-specific content.
  • Three silos, not one. Repatriation + cremation transfer + ashes transport. Three times the keyword surface area.
  • Guide layer. "What to do if someone dies in [country]" pages are informational magnets that feed into our service pages. RBI has nothing here.
  • FAQ-first strategy. Every page gets 6-8 FAQs targeting People Also Ask. With schema. RBI has zero.
  • City pages. Bangkok, Malaga, Phuket, Tenerife. Where people actually die abroad. RBI has zero city-level content.

Respect Their Strengths

  • Don't compete on brand authority (yet). 55 years vs day 1. We compete on content quality and page-level relevance, not domain authority.
  • Don't display pricing initially. RBI shows prices. We can't match their specificity without a pricing model. Better to show cost guidance ranges and factors that drive price.
  • Don't fragment the audience. RBI tries to serve 7 audiences. We serve one: the family. Everything flows from that decision.
  • Accreditations matter. Plan to acquire relevant certifications as soon as the business is operational. SAIF membership, for example.

Build Over Time

  • Phase 1-2: Content quality wins. Outrank thin pages with depth.
  • Phase 3-4: Coverage wins. Match and exceed their 150 countries.
  • Phase 5+: Authority wins. Backlinks, press coverage, testimonials, accreditations build domain authority to compete head-to-head even on brand queries.

Monitor

  • Watch for RBI content update. If they hire a content team and bulk-update their 150 country pages, the window narrows. Track their sitemap for changes.
  • Track new entrants. If another SEO-savvy operator launches a content-rich repatriation site, we need to know immediately.
  • GOV.UK changes. If GOV.UK expands their "death abroad" content with country-specific pages, it shifts the informational landscape. They rank #1 for generic queries already.
  • Major funeral chains. If Dignity or Co-op invest in repatriation SEO, they bring significant domain authority. Low probability but high impact.

Bottom Line

The market leader has 150 country pages with 50 words each. No guides, no FAQs, no schema, no city pages, no separate silos for cremation/ashes. Their brand is strong but their content is weak. Every piece of our build plan directly targets a gap they left open. The competitive environment has rarely been more favourable for a content-led market entry.
