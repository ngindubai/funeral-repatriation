Spain

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Updated: 21 Aug 2025

1. Registration

  • Death must be registered at the local civil registry (Registro Civil)
  • Funeral director normally handles registration on the family's behalf
  • Documents needed: birth certificate or details, passport, spouse details if married
  • Marriage certificate, divorce papers if applicable

2. Death Certificate

  • International death certificate available: "certificado plurilingue" (multilingual)
  • Spanish death certificates do NOT show cause of death
  • 3 free copies provided initially
  • Digital certificates available from Ministry of Justice website
  • Further copies available from any Civil Registry or online

3. Post Mortem

  • Post mortem ordered if cause of death is unknown or unnatural
  • Conducted by forensic authorities
  • Reports may take time to be released to next of kin

4. Repatriation

  • Funeral director arranges all documentation for body transfer
  • Need death certificate and embalming certificate
  • Funeral director coordinates transport with UK funeral director

5. Cremation and Ashes

  • Cremation is legal and available in Spain
  • To transport ashes out of Spain: need death certificate, cremation certificate, and local authority permission certificate
  • Airlines must be notified in advance

6. Local Burial

  • Burial is available through local funeral director
  • Funeral director handles all local arrangements
  • Do not cremate abroad if a coroner in England/Wales may need to hold an inquest
FCDO Emergency: +44 (0)20 7008 5000

France

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Updated: 28 Jan 2021

1. Registration

  • Death must be registered at the local mairie (town hall)
  • Registration must happen within 24 hours
  • Need: identity document (passport), proof of residence, witness details
  • Hospital or medical professional usually initiates the process

2. Death Certificate

  • Acte de deces issued by the mairie
  • Multilingual translation possible on request
  • French death certificates do NOT show cause of death
  • Multiple copies can be requested at any time from the mairie

3. Post Mortem

  • Post mortem conducted at Institut Medico Legal (IML) if required
  • Ordered in cases of violent, sudden, or unexplained death
  • Tissue samples and organs may be retained for testing
  • Reports can take months to be released

4. Repatriation

  • Need: death certificate, embalming certificate, permission to transfer
  • French funeral director coordinates with UK funeral director
  • Embalming is required for international transport of remains

5. Cremation and Ashes

  • Cremation is legal and available in France
  • To transport ashes out of France: need local authority certificate
  • Airlines must be informed in advance
  • Do not cremate if a coroner inquest may follow in England/Wales

6. Support Network

  • Bereavement Support Network (BSN) based in France helps English speakers
  • Voluntary organisation offering practical and emotional support
  • Can assist with navigating French bureaucracy in English

Portugal

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Published: 21 Mar 2025

1. Registration

  • Death must be registered at the Portuguese registry office (Conservatoria do Registo Civil)
  • Funeral director can normally handle registration on family's behalf
  • Documents needed: birth details, parents' names, civil status, passport
  • Spouse details, marriage certificate, divorce papers if applicable

2. Death Certificate

  • Assento de Obito issued after registration
  • International version available: Certidao de Obito Internacional (multilingual/English translation)
  • Portuguese death certificates do NOT show cause of death
  • Extra copies recommended as you will need them later
  • Further copies from any Civil Registration Office in Portugal or Portuguese Consulate
  • Online viewing available with 6-month check code via GOV.UK step-by-step guide

3. Post Mortem

  • Required if cause of death is unknown, unnatural, sudden, or violent
  • Compulsory inquiry conducted by public prosecutor for suspicious deaths, accidents (including road traffic)
  • Post mortem normally within 3-4 working days of death
  • Cultural/religious sensitivities may NOT be considered
  • Tissue samples and organs may be removed without permission (normally returned before body release)
  • PM reports not given to next of kin automatically. Email prosecutor with passport copy to request.
  • Prosecutor releases report only when inquiry closed. Can take up to 12 months.

4. Obtaining Cause of Death

  • Death after long illness (no legal PM): apply to hospital administration for declaration or medical records
  • Sudden death (legal PM): apply to Public Prosecutor for PM report and court conclusion
  • Enquiry takes several months. Report not released until Prosecutor concludes.

5. Burial and Cremation

  • Local funeral director required for burial or cremation
  • Can instruct funeral director remotely (form + passport copy needed)
  • Funerals normally within 72 hours of death, or 48 hours after post mortem
  • May need permission from public prosecutor for cremation
  • Do NOT cremate abroad if you want a coroner inquest in England/Wales
  • If nobody takes responsibility, local authorities arrange a pauper's burial

6. Repatriation and Ashes

  • Funeral director arranges bringing body to UK
  • Ashes: need death certificate, cremation certificate, follow Portuguese regs
  • Tell airline in advance if carrying ashes
  • Ashes can also be shipped by air freight (expensive). Check customs duty on UK arrival.
  • Personal belongings may be kept as evidence during inquiry
  • Consulate cannot collect, store, or forward belongings
Online Certificates GOV.UK publishes a step-by-step guide for ordering Portuguese certificates online, including a 6-month check code for digital verification.
FCDO Emergency: +44 (0)20 7008 5000
Embassy Lisbon: Rua de Sao Bernardo 33, 1249-082 Lisbon
Vice Consulate: Portimao (Algarve)

Italy

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Updated: 18 Oct 2024

1. Registration

  • Death registered at the Comune (town hall/municipality)
  • Hospital deaths: hospital usually handles auto-registration with the Comune
  • Deaths outside hospital: police or family report to the Comune
  • Next of kin priority: spouse, children, parents, then siblings

2. Death Certificate

  • Standard death certificate does NOT show cause of death
  • ISTAT certificate (for statistical purposes) does show cause
  • Multilingual death certificate available on request
  • Copies available from the Comune where death was registered

3. Post Mortem

  • Ordered by prosecutor (Procura della Repubblica) if death is suspicious or unexplained
  • 24-hour mandatory wait before body preparation can begin
  • Tissue samples and organs may be retained
  • Reports can take months to be released

4. Repatriation

  • Nulla Osta (transfer certificate) required for repatriation
  • Repatriation typically takes 3-10 days to arrange
  • Basic preservative treatment required (full embalming optional)
  • Zinc-lined coffin required for international transport
  • Funeral director coordinates documentation and logistics

5. Burial in Italy

  • Burial commonly in above-ground niches (tumulazione) rather than below-ground
  • Burial rights typically 20-30 years (must be renewed or remains moved)
  • Costs and availability vary by region and municipality

6. Cremation

  • Cremation increasingly common in Italy
  • Crematoriums not available in every region
  • Most crematoriums lack ceremony facilities
  • Cremation authorisation requires proof it was deceased's expressed wish (written statement, will, or family declaration)
Healthcare Costs GHIC/EHIC card may cover hospital costs incurred before death. Legal aid available in Italy if income is below EUR 12,838.01/year.
24-Hour Wait Rule Italian law requires a 24-hour waiting period after death before any body preparation (embalming, dressing) can begin.
FCDO Emergency: +44 (0)20 7008 5000

Greece

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Updated: 30 Jan 2026

1. Registration

  • Death registered at the local dimos (municipality)
  • Must be registered within 30 days
  • Hospital or doctor provides medical certificate of death

2. Death Certificate

  • Certificate issued in Greek: lixiarxiki praxi thanatou
  • No multilingual version. Translation must be arranged separately.
  • Usually DOES show cause of death (unlike most EU countries)
  • Copies available from the dimos

3. Post Mortem

  • Post mortem ordered by prosecutor if death is sudden, unexplained, or suspicious
  • Post mortem reports can take months to over a year to be released
  • Significant delays are common in the Greek system

4. Repatriation

  • Local formalities typically take 8-10 days
  • Documents required: death certificate, coroner permission, passport, embalming certificate
  • Funeral director coordinates with Greek and UK authorities

5. Burial in Greece

  • Local burial available through Greek funeral director
  • In some cemeteries, remains are EXHUMED after 3 years and moved to an ossuary
  • This is standard Greek practice, not optional in many areas

6. Cremation

  • Cremation is now legal in Greece
  • Availability may be limited. Athens has a crematorium.
  • Ashes transport requires death certificate and cremation certificate
Exhumation After 3 Years In many Greek cemeteries, burial is temporary. Remains are exhumed after approximately 3 years and placed in an ossuary. Families need to be aware of this before choosing local burial.
FCDO Emergency: +44 (0)20 7008 5000

Turkey

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Updated: 5 Oct 2023

1. Registration

  • Hospital deaths: hospital records the death
  • Non-hospital deaths: forensic institute or prosecutor handles initial recording
  • Death report (Olum Belgesi) sent to registry office (Nufus Dairesi)
  • Registry office issues the official death certificate

2. Death Certificate

  • Formul C death certificate is bilingual: Turkish and English
  • Formul C does NOT show cause of death
  • This is the document needed for repatriation and UK processes

3. Post Mortem

  • Post mortem ordered by prosecutor if death is unexplained or suspicious
  • Post mortem reports are NOT given to next of kin
  • To obtain: must apply through the Turkish court system
  • Can be a lengthy and complex process

4. Repatriation

  • Documents required: Formul C death certificate, embalming certificate, permission to transfer
  • Funeral director coordinates all paperwork
  • Embalming required for international transport
CREMATION IS NOT LEGAL IN TURKEY There are no cremation facilities in Turkey. The body must either be repatriated or buried locally. This is a strict legal prohibition, not a practical limitation.
Post Mortem Reports Unlike most countries, Turkish authorities do not release post mortem reports to the family. Access requires a formal application through the court system.
FCDO Emergency: +44 (0)20 7008 5000

Thailand

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Updated: 20 Nov 2024

1. Registration

  • Deaths outside hospital: report to police first
  • Register at local district office (amphur)
  • Only ONE original death certificate issued per registration
  • Certificate requires Ministry of Foreign Affairs certification for use outside Thailand

2. Death Certificate

  • Issued in Thai. No English version available from authorities.
  • Does NOT show cause of death
  • Only one original copy. Guard it carefully.
  • Needs MFA certification before it is valid for UK processes

3. Post Mortem

  • Post mortem normally required for non-Thai nationals
  • Post mortem report takes approximately 45 business days
  • Conducted by police forensic surgeon
  • Significant delays are normal

4. Repatriation

  • Embassy letter needed to release body from Thai authorities (online application)
  • Embalming required for international transport
  • Funeral director coordinates all documents and logistics
  • Ashes do NOT need an Embassy letter (only the body does)

5. Local Burial

  • Local burial is rare, expensive, and typically restricted to religious grounds
  • Most foreigners choose repatriation or cremation

6. Cremation

  • Cremation is widely available in Thailand
  • Common choice for families who prefer not to repatriate remains
  • Ashes can be brought to UK without Embassy letter
30-Day Deadline If no arrangements are made within 30 days, Thai authorities may proceed with a pauper's mass funeral. Families must act promptly.
One Original Certificate Only Thailand issues only ONE original death certificate. Unlike most countries there is no way to get additional originals. Keep it secure and use certified copies.
British Embassy Bangkok (Emergency): +66 (0)2 305 8333
FCDO Emergency: +44 (0)20 7008 5000

United States

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Updated: 13 Mar 2026

1. Registration

  • Funeral home or medical professional registers the death
  • Medical examiner handles sudden, unexpected, or suspicious deaths
  • Process varies by state. Each state has its own vital records office.

2. Death Certificate

  • US death certificates DO show cause of death (unlike most other countries)
  • Issued in English
  • Multiple certified copies available from state vital records office
  • May need apostille for international use

3. Post Mortem / Autopsy

  • Autopsy ordered by medical examiner if cause of death is unknown
  • Family may have access to autopsy reports (varies by state law)
  • Some states allow family to request an independent autopsy
  • Coroner/medical examiner system varies by county/state

4. Repatriation

  • Documents needed: death certificate, embalming certificate, cremation certificate (if applicable)
  • US funeral director coordinates with UK funeral director
  • Embalming required for international transport

5. Cremation and Ashes

  • Cremation widely available across the US
  • Ashes: use non-metallic container (so it can pass through airport x-ray)
  • Tell airline in advance if carrying ashes

6. Financial

  • County or state may arrange burial if family cannot pay
  • US healthcare costs before death can be extremely high
  • Travel insurance is critical. Without it, costs fall to the family.
State-by-State Variation The US has no single national process. Laws, timelines, costs, and procedures vary by state. Families need state-specific guidance, not just "USA" information.
FCDO Emergency: +44 (0)20 7008 5000

India

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Updated: 23 Dec 2024

1. Registration

  • Two-step process: (1) medical cause of death certificate from doctor/hospital, (2) register with local municipal authority
  • Death certificate issued in 10-15 working days after registration
  • Death certificate does NOT normally show cause of death

2. Death Certificate

  • Issued in local language (Hindi or state language)
  • Does not normally show cause of death
  • 10-15 working day turnaround
  • Translation may be needed for UK processes

3. Post Mortem

  • Post mortem conducted by police forensic surgeon
  • Retained organs are NOT returned to the family
  • Forensic test results can take 6 months to several years
  • Extremely long delays are considered normal

4. Repatriation

  • Embalming required for international transport
  • Repatriation process takes approximately 2 weeks
  • Limited mortuary space. Pressure to make prompt decisions.
  • Funeral director handles documentation and logistics

5. Cremation

  • Cremation widely available (wooden pyre or electric crematorium)
  • Religious and traditional cremation on open pyre is common
  • Ashes must be sent to UK by air freight only (cannot carry on person)

6. Legal Considerations

  • Indian law does NOT recognise same-sex partners for next-of-kin purposes
  • This may affect who can authorise decisions about the body
  • Legal advice recommended for unmarried or same-sex partners
Retained Organs Not Returned If a post mortem is conducted in India, any organs or tissue samples retained for testing are NOT returned to the family. This is a fixed policy, not negotiable.
Limited Mortuary Space Morgue facilities in India are limited. Authorities will push for a quick decision on cremation, burial, or repatriation. Families need to be prepared to decide quickly.
FCDO Emergency: +44 (0)20 7008 5000

Egypt

Source: GOV.UK FCDO | Updated: 18 Oct 2024

1. Registration

  • Death registered at the district health office
  • Death certificate issued in Arabic only
  • Professional translation required for UK use

2. Death Certificate

  • Issued in Arabic only. No multilingual version.
  • Does not show cause of death in a format useful to UK authorities
  • Translation and possible apostille/legalisation needed

3. Post Mortem

  • Post mortem can be conducted WITHOUT the family's consent
  • Organs may be retained for testing
  • Families have limited control over this process
  • Mortuary facilities are variable in quality across Egypt

4. Repatriation

  • Embassy permission letter required for BOTH burial and repatriation
  • Embalming required for international transport
  • Funeral director handles coordination with Egyptian and UK authorities

5. Cremation

  • Cremation is technically legal but practically unavailable
  • Only one unofficial crematorium exists (in Alexandria)
  • Repatriation or local burial are the realistic options
  • Egypt is a Muslim-majority country where cremation is not culturally practised

6. Local Burial

  • Christian cemeteries available in Cairo: Latin Cemetery, Protestant Cemetery
  • Embassy permission letter required even for local burial
  • Muslim burial also available if family requests
Embassy Permission Required for Everything Unlike most countries, Egypt requires a British Embassy permission letter for both repatriation AND local burial. Nothing proceeds without it.
Cremation Practically Unavailable Despite being technically legal, cremation is not a realistic option in Egypt. The only crematorium is unofficial and in Alexandria. Plan for repatriation or local burial.
