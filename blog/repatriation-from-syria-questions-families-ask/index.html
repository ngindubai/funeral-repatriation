Syria: Why Some Cases Aren't Yet Possible

The Embassy in Damascus has been shut since 2012, and the transition since Assad's fall shapes every case. Contact us any hour.

A death in Syria is one of the hardest cases a UK family can face right now, and it has nothing to do with paperwork alone. The Embassy in Damascus has been closed since 2012. Since December 2024, the country has been in political transition. Both facts change what’s actually possible before any documentation step begins.

Call the FCDO before anything else

Ring the FCDO Emergency Travel Line first: 020 7008 5000, available 24 hours. The British Embassy in Beirut (+961 1 960 800) provides what consular help is currently possible from outside the country. Their guidance on what’s achievable in a specific part of Syria will be more current than anything published here.

Three administrations, one country

When Assad’s government fell, Hayat Tahrir al-Sham and allied factions took Damascus and most of western Syria. The Kurdish-led Syrian Democratic Forces still run the north-east. Former regime areas along the Alawite coast operate under their own local dynamics. Civil registration offices that answered to the old government are being folded into new structures, and in some places that process simply hasn’t finished. A firm with current Syria contacts is the only realistic source of up-to-date guidance, because what was true last month may not hold this month.

Documentation reflects this patchwork. Damascus and the main cities have the most advanced reconstitution of civil administration; Aleppo, Homs, and Deir ez-Zor carry severe infrastructure damage from over a decade of conflict; the SDF-controlled north-east runs on a separate track entirely; rural areas may have no working documentation process at all.

Getting a body out of the country

Damascus Airport resumed limited commercial flights after the transition, with Turkish Airlines and a handful of Middle Eastern carriers cautiously restoring service. Schedules aren’t reliable yet and cargo acceptance for human remains isn’t confirmed as routine. Most repatriation now runs overland to Beirut first, then air cargo from Beirut International Airport through Middle East hubs to the UK. That overland leg depends on safe passage through Syrian territory, which can’t be assumed for every route or every day.

Being honest about timelines

Any firm promising a fixed date for a Syria case isn’t being straight with you. Some cases genuinely aren’t achievable yet, not because of missing paperwork but because the administration that would issue it doesn’t function reliably in that location. The FCDO can tell you what’s realistic where the death occurred. Repatriate Service keeps regional contacts across the Middle East and will give you the same honest picture.
