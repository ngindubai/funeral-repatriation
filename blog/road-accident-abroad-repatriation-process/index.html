Road Accident Abroad: The Repatriation Process

Road traffic deaths abroad almost always trigger a police investigation before repatriation can begin. What that means for timing. Contact us 24/7.

A road accident abroad puts a family through two processes at once: the practical work of repatriation, and a police investigation that has to run its course first.

Why the investigation comes first

Almost every country treats a road traffic death as an accidental death requiring formal investigation, whatever the circumstances. Local police attend the scene, prepare a report, and in most jurisdictions the body goes through a post-mortem examination before a death certificate can be issued. None of this is optional, and none of it can be sped up by asking.

The complexity of the accident affects how long this takes. A single-vehicle accident with a clear cause tends to clear faster than a multi-vehicle collision with several casualties, where the investigation has more to establish. As a rough guide, expect the investigation stage to add 1 to 4 weeks to the timeline compared with an uncomplicated natural death.

The funeral director’s role during this stage

There isn’t much for the family to do while the local investigation is open, and that’s often the hardest part. A funeral director experienced in international cases will typically stay in contact with the local police or prosecutor’s office, prepare the documentation that will be needed once release is authorised, and flag to the family as soon as there’s movement.

This is also the stage where the British Embassy can help, though only within its usual limits. Consular staff can’t investigate or influence the outcome, but they can advise on how the local system works and connect the family with an English-speaking lawyer if the circumstances call for one.

What happens once the body is released

Once local police and any prosecutor sign off, the standard repatriation documentation proceeds: the death certificate, its certified translation, an export permit, and preparation of the body to international transport standards. From here the process looks much like any other repatriation.

The UK coroner and the inquest

Road accidents fall squarely into the category of violent or unnatural deaths that England and Wales coroners investigate under the Coroners and Justice Act 2009. Expect an inquest to be opened once the body is repatriated. This doesn’t delay the funeral: the coroner issues an interim certificate of death once the inquest opens, and that certificate is enough to register the death for practical purposes, start probate, and satisfy most banks. The full inquest, examining exactly what happened, can run for months and concludes separately with a final certificate.

Scotland handles this through the Procurator Fiscal and a Fatal Accident Inquiry rather than a coroner’s inquest; the practical effect for families, an interim period followed by a formal hearing, is similar.

If fault lies with another party

Where local investigators attribute the accident to another driver, a separate legal process, criminal, civil, or both, may run in the country where it happened. This sits alongside, not instead of, the UK coroner’s inquest. Families considering a compensation claim should speak to a solicitor who specifically handles cross-border road traffic cases, since the applicable law and time limits vary significantly by country.
