Death in Hospital Abroad: The Release Procedure

A hospital death abroad is certified faster than most, but release can stall over an unpaid bill. What families need to check first. Contact us 24/7.

A hospital is, in one respect, the easiest place for a death abroad to happen. The doctor already knows the patient. The cause of death is documented. There’s no police report to wait for, no forensic examiner to book in.

That’s the good news. The complication is different, and it catches families off guard: the hospital’s own billing department.

Why the medical side moves quickly

When someone dies under a doctor’s care, with a known diagnosis and a visible treatment history, most countries’ medical certification is straightforward. The attending physician signs the cause of death. There’s rarely a need for the police or forensic involvement that an unattended death elsewhere would trigger. Compare this to a sudden collapse in a hotel room with no witnesses, where authorities typically want confirmation before they’ll certify anything.

Where it actually gets stuck

Some hospitals abroad will not release a body until the account is settled, or until an insurer has confirmed it will pay. This isn’t unusual, and it isn’t personal. It’s standard billing practice in health systems where private hospitals expect payment before, not after, they hand over records or remains.

Find the travel insurance policy immediately. Check the deceased’s phone, wallet, and email for a confirmation, and check any premium bank account or credit card that bundles travel cover. Give the hospital’s billing office the insurer’s direct line as soon as you have it. An insurer confirming cover in writing, even before the final bill is agreed, is often enough to move things along.

If there’s no insurance, the hospital will usually still discuss the account with the family directly. It’s a conversation worth having early rather than late.

What the consulate can and cannot do

The FCDO does not pay medical bills, hospital fees, or the cost of repatriation. It will not settle an account on the family’s behalf. What it can do is contact the hospital administration to confirm next of kin, register the death consularly, and, in genuine hardship, consider a discretionary grant. None of this replaces sorting the insurance question directly with the hospital.

The paper trail

You’ll need the medical certificate of cause of death, the civil death certificate once registered, and, where the hospital runs its own mortuary, an internal release authorisation. Anything not in English needs certified translation before it has any standing with UK authorities.
