Yemen: What's Possible When the Embassy Closed in 2015

Two administrations, one closed UK embassy, and a case that still needs an honest answer. Contact us any hour.

Yemen has been at war since 2014, when the Houthi movement seized Sana’a and forced the internationally recognised government into exile, effectively to Riyadh. The UK Embassy in Sana’a closed in 2015 and has not reopened. This is one of the two or three most difficult repatriation destinations anywhere in the world, and any family facing it deserves an honest assessment of what’s achievable, not false reassurance.

Call the FCDO immediately on 020 7008 5000, available 24 hours. The British Embassy in Riyadh, Saudi Arabia, provides what limited consular assistance is currently possible.

Which administration controls the ground

Yemen’s split matters enormously for what happens next. In IRG-controlled areas, Aden, Hadramawt, Marib, and the south and east generally, documentation goes through the internationally recognised government’s civil authorities, and Aden International Airport is the main route out. This is the more tractable scenario.

In Houthi-controlled areas, Sana’a, Hudaydah, Sa’dah, and the north-west, documentation runs through Houthi authorities instead, and Sana’a Airport offers very limited, unpredictable scheduled service. This is significantly harder.

Active frontline areas are a different question entirely. Repatriation simply isn’t achievable there. No specialist firm can promise otherwise.

Islamic burial and the pressure of time

Yemen is almost entirely Muslim, and burial within 24 hours is universally practised. If repatriation is intended, that intention needs to be communicated within hours of learning of the death, not days. Islamic scholars do permit delay for a family based abroad, but the family still has to act without delay in making that intention known.

Documents, and who issues them

The death certificate comes from whichever authority controls the area, IRG or Houthi, and it will be in Arabic, requiring certified translation. An export permit follows from the same authority. An embalming certificate may or may not be available, depending on location. Consular registration through the British Embassy in Riyadh is subject to current capability at the time of the case.

Getting out

Aden International Airport, under IRG administration, is the main accessible gateway, with Flydubai, Air Arabia, and Yemenia running limited services via Dubai and Amman. Sana’a International Airport, under Houthi control, has very limited and unpredictable scheduled service. Seiyun Airport in the Hadhramaut region has limited access, used mostly for humanitarian or charter operations. Every route is subject to frequent change, and cargo acceptance for human remains needs advance confirmation with the carrier.

Cremation isn’t practised in Yemen. Full body repatriation is the only option.
