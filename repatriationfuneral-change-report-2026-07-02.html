SEO Build: Change Report

Repatriate Service (repatriationfuneral.com) · 2 July 2026 · Every change made in this build, and why

This documents the work that turned the SEO audit of 29 June / 2 July into shipped changes. All 13 audit findings plus the llms.txt work were built, verified, and deployed to the live site (master) across 15 commits, one deploy per block so no two deploys clobbered each other. Every content and template change passed the site's QA gate, schema check, and title check before going live. Where a change involved a judgement call (prices, author identity, the answer-first layout), the decision is recorded with its trade-off. Where verification caught a mistake mid-build, that is recorded too, because catching them is the point of verifying.

How to read this

Changes are grouped into the four blocks they shipped in. Each item states what changed, the file(s), why, and any trade-off or mistake caught during verification. The audit finding IDs (F1 to F13) are kept so this maps back to the audit report.

Block 1 Discoverability

Making the site, and the route matrix inside it, easier for search engines and AI systems to find and read.

llms.txt and llms-full.txt upgraded

Filessite/static/llms.txt, site/static/llms-full.txt What
  • Removed every GBP price figure (for example "GBP 3,000-6,000") from both files.
  • Removed the em dashes both files carried (the ban is absolute).
  • Corrected the stale "26 countries" framing and added a country-to-country route-corridor section pointing at /routes/.
Why. Other AI systems (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude) read these files and they drive real traffic. The audit had suggested de-prioritising them; that was overridden because they earn citations even though Google's own AI Overview ranking does not read them. Prices were removed to match the site-wide no-figures decision (see F13).

F7: country hubs now link down into the route matrix

Filesnew site/layouts/partials/countries/routes-from.html; called from country-hub.html and the four variant partials country-b/c/d/e.html What Each country hub now shows a "Repatriation routes from {country}" block linking every built route page for that origin. 189 of 238 hubs now carry the block (the rest have no built outbound routes yet and fill in as the matrix grows).
Why. Routes linked up to their hub, but hubs never linked down, so most of the 2,467 route pages were reachable only through the sitemap. This deepens the silo and gives crawlers a clean path into the matrix, which matters more while Google's indexing report is lagging.
Caught during the build. A naive slug match would have silently dropped routes: the USA hub is keyed usa but its routes use origin_slug: united-states (and some usa). Switched to matching on a normalised origin_name instead, which captures both. Also discovered hubs use variant dispatch (variant A renders inline, B to E via separate partials), so the block was built once as a shared partial and called from all five rather than pasted into each.

Block 2 Trust and schema

Making the structured data honest and the trust signals defensible on a life-and-death (YMYL) topic.

F5: duplicate Organization schema removed from route pages

Filesite/layouts/routes/single.html What Route pages emitted the Organization block twice (once site-wide, once in the route template). Removed the route-level copy; the site-wide one in baseof.html is the single source.
Why. One Organization entity per page is correct; the duplicate was redundant and thinner. No trade-off.

F2: mismatched FAQ schema removed from country hubs

Filesite/layouts/countries/country-hub.html What The FAQ structured data wrote the same boilerplate answer ("contact our specialist team...") to every question, while the visible page showed real answers. Removed the FAQPage markup entirely.
Why. A schema answer must match the visible answer; a mismatch across the hubs is a manual-action risk on a YMYL site. Google restricted FAQ rich results to government and health sites in 2023, so the markup earned almost nothing anyway. Removing it ends the risk at effectively zero cost, rather than reconciling five different visible-FAQ layouts against one schema block.
Trade-off. The hubs lose FAQ rich-result eligibility, but for this sector that eligibility was already gone. The visible FAQ on the page is unchanged.

F13: prices removed from country hubs (qualitative guidance only)

Filesite/layouts/countries/country-hub.html What Removed every price output (hero cost stat, answer-brief cost line, the cost banner and full breakdown table, two priced FAQ answers, the ashes cost cards). Replaced with a "what the cost depends on" factor list plus a route to the contact form.
Why. The site's own rule is "no prices, ever", which the route pages already followed, but the hubs did not. A quoted range can mislead a family whose case does not fit it. Decision taken with Gareth: qualitative guidance only, no figures.
Caught during the build. Prices came from three places, not one: the template, the country data, and free-text inside two data fields (cost_notes, insurance_note) that hid GBP figures for several countries. All three were handled. A first sweep flagged 34 hub files as still carrying prices, but that turned out to be a false positive (the pattern was matching population and altitude numbers like "2,400 metres", not money). A currency-anchored re-check confirmed zero real price figures remained.

F3: author identity and entity trust (E-E-A-T)

Filesbaseof.html, country-hub.html, partials/countries/country-e.html, blog/single.html What
  • Site-wide Organization gained areaServed and knowsAbout (a service-area business, modelled with no invented postal address).
  • The fabricated "Senior Repatriation Consultant" Person was replaced by the Organization (plus a reviewedBy credit) in both schema and the visible byline on variant-E hubs.
  • Blog Article schema gained a reviewedBy Organization.
Why. This is a death, legal and consular topic where trust signals matter. Decision taken with Gareth (Option B): keep the Organization as the author everywhere and strengthen the honest signals, rather than invent named experts.
Trade-off, stated plainly. Named, credentialed authors are a stronger E-E-A-T signal than an Organization, but the site's personas are fictional, and attaching invented credentials to YMYL content is itself a trust risk that Google warns about. So no fabricated experts, addresses, or reviews were added. If real people with truthful bios ever exist, that stronger version can be built then.
Discovered. The blog persona names (James Whitfield and the others) are dormant frontmatter, never rendered and never in schema, so there was no visible fake byline on the blog to reconcile and no conflict with the house persona rule. Left untouched.

Block 3 Answer-first content

Getting the direct answer high in the page, and fixing thin, ungrammatical prose.

F1: the direct-answer block moved above the overview

Filesite/layouts/routes/single.html What On route variants B and E, the answer block was rendering after the overview narrative. Moved it above the overview so all five variants now lead with the answer.
Why. AI Overviews and featured snippets pull from pages that answer the query directly, high up. Putting the answer first gives every page its best chance of being cited.
Corrected against the code. The plan assumed variant D also needed fixing; reading the actual template showed D was already answer-first, so it was left alone. Variants A and C were already correct too. Only B and E needed the swap.

F4: 501 thin, ungrammatical route overviews rewritten

Filesnew f4_thicken_overview.py; 501 route .md files (the overview_body field only) What Many route overviews were a broken 3-sentence template (for example "The [authority] takes jurisdiction when the death is: violent, suspicious, or unexplained deaths"). Rewrote them into 85 to 115 word, grammatical overviews composed only from facts already in the same file (emergency number, registration authority, document time, the jurisdiction rule with its grammar fixed, the standard air-cargo process, and the timeline). Three sentence frames rotate by a hash of the slug so pages do not read identically.

Why. Thin, broken auto-generated text is the exact vulnerability a spam update targets, and it reads badly on a sensitive topic. Every other element of these pages was strong, so this was the weak link.
Trade-off and deliberate stop. Only the 501 pages that exactly match the canonical thin template were rewritten. The remaining roughly 300 thinner-but-differently-phrased pages were left untouched rather than risk corrupting them; they are a noted future batch. No new facts were invented; the composition can only use facts already present, which is safer for YMYL than free text generation.
Caught during the sample check (before any bulk change). Five issues were found and fixed on a hand-checked sample first: lowercasing corrupted proper nouns ("the botswana Police Service"), a missing article ("registered with Registrar General"), one frame ran to 120 words, nested parentheses in an emergency number broke the capture, and a loose match would have destroyed extra facts on richer files (Zimbabwe's apostille note). Tightening to an exact-template match resolved the last three. Zero new QA failures resulted.

Block 4 On-page tidy and governance

SERP display fixes, clearing the backlog of QA failures, and recording rules for future runs.

F8: rendered page titles shortened

Filesroutes/single.html, country-hub.html What Dropped the " | Repatriate Service" suffix from route and hub titles.
Why. The suffix pushed the rendered title past the point Google truncates in results (2,033 of 2,467 route titles were over 60 characters). The bare titles were already fine on their own, so dropping the suffix fixed the display for over 2,000 routes and roughly 233 hubs without rewriting any title text.
Trade-off. Slightly less brand reinforcement in the tag, but it was being truncated anyway. Five hubs with very long official country names (for example Saint Helena, Ascension and Tristan da Cunha) remain marginally over; left rather than redesigning the title format.

F9: all 36 pre-existing QA failures cleared

Files36 route .md files What Trimmed 33 over-length meta descriptions and 3 over-length titles, and replaced the banned word "vital" in 6 files. Result: the QA gate went from 36 failures to 0 (2,467 pass).
Why. These failures pre-dated this work but blocked a clean QA run. Descriptions over 155 characters truncate in results; the banned-word list is house style.
How the "vital" fix kept accuracy. The flagged text was "vital statistics office" / "vital records office", the correct legal name for the Canadian, Nigerian and US registration agencies. Rather than degrade the wording, it was replaced with the equally accurate "civil registration office", matching a substitution the project had already used in earlier sessions.
Caught during verification. Two of the 33 description trims accidentally dropped the "Contact us" call to action while shortening. Re-running the title check surfaced it, and a second pass restored the CTA on both. This is why the gate was re-run after the change rather than assumed clean.

F6: social share image and Twitter card added

Filesite/layouts/_default/baseof.html What The site had no og:image or Twitter card at all. Added both site-wide, placed so every page type inherits them.
Why. Controls how a page looks when a grieving family shares a link, and some AI and social surfaces read these tags. A single calm, small-file image is used site-wide for reliable rendering.
Honest note. F6 was in the audit but slipped through the gap between the audit's numbered findings and the four thematic blocks. It was caught on an end-of-session completeness re-check and then completed. A purpose-built 1200x630 branded image would render larger than the current default and can be swapped in later with no other change.

F11 and F10: governance rules recorded

FileMEMORY.md What Two permanent build decisions were written in so every future autonomous run reads them: (F11) do not buy brand mentions or use private blog networks to influence AI citations; (F10) keep and maintain llms.txt as a live deliverable.
Why. F11 is a guard-rail: the paid-mention tactic is a Google violation and does not work on its current AI Overview system anyway; the correct path is organic rank plus direct-answer structure, which this build already strengthened. F10 records the override of the audit's llms.txt advice.

F12: quarterly fact-refresh spec written

Filenew CONTENT-REFRESH-SPEC.md What A spec for a recurring pass that re-verifies dated regulatory facts (the FCDO emergency line, embassy operational status, travel advisories, treaty membership) against named government sources and flags changes for confirmation before applying.
Why. These facts go stale, and a wrong consular fact causes real harm. The spec's core rule is "flag, never blind-apply": every change cites a named dated source and is confirmed by a person or Opus; unverifiable facts are flagged, never guessed. It also fixes a subtle integrity issue found in the audit, where the site stamps "Updated [current month]" on every build with no actual review behind it, by tying that date to a real review marker.

What was deliberately left, and why

  • ~300 differently-phrased thin overviews (F4): left untouched rather than risk corrupting their sourced facts. A future batch with its own pattern analysis.
  • 5 hubs with very long country names (F8): marginally over the title length; not worth redesigning the title scheme for.
  • 27 pre-existing "no call-to-action" description warnings (lower severity than the 36 failures, and outside this task's scope).
  • A purpose-built 1200x630 share image (F6): the wired default works; a branded image is an optional design upgrade.
  • Off-site validation and monitoring: schema and social-card validation on live URLs, Search Console reindex and query tracking, and the first run of the F12 fact-check. These need logged-in third-party tools, not repository changes.
