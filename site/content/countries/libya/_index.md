Libya is in a different category from any other repatriation destination this service handles. There is no functioning national government with consistent authority across the country. There is no resident British Embassy. Air access is limited and unreliable. Armed groups control significant territory. And yet British nationals die in Libya — oil workers, journalists, NGO staff, private security contractors — and their families need to bring them home.

This page explains what the process actually looks like. It is not straightforward, and we will not pretend otherwise.

## The political reality and why it matters for repatriation

Since 2014, Libya has had competing governments and multiple armed factions controlling different parts of the country. The Government of National Unity (GNU) nominally controls Tripoli and the west. The Libyan National Army (LNA) controls much of the east, including Benghazi. The south — Fezzan — has minimal central government presence and significant activity from armed groups including remnants of Islamic State.

For repatriation, this means:
- The authority that can issue a death certificate and authorise body movement depends on where the death occurred
- There is no single Ministry of Health to issue an export permit
- A specialist who has worked in Libya recently and has current contacts with both GNU and LNA networks is essential

## British consular support: what is possible

The British Embassy in Tripoli closed in 2014. Consular services for British nationals in Libya are provided remotely by British Embassy Tunis (Tunisia). This means:
- No UK consul can physically attend the scene of a death in Libya
- Consular registration is handled remotely via phone and email
- If a family member wants to enter Libya to assist — this is not advisable, but sometimes attempted — they do so without UK consular protection in-country

The FCDO Emergency Travel Line handles all Libya consular enquiries: +44 207 008 5000.

## Who is in Libya and why

British nationals present in Libya at any given time typically include:
- Oil and gas workers: operating in secure compounds, often with BP, TotalEnergies, and specialist contractors in the Sirte Basin and offshore Kwanza Basin
- Journalists and documentary filmmakers covering the conflict
- NGO and humanitarian staff operating in Benghazi, Derna, and Misrata
- Private security contractors providing protection to embassies, oil facilities, or corporate clients
- Members of the large British-Libyan diaspora visiting family

Each category has different insurance and employer support arrangements. Oil company workers almost always have repatriation insurance through their employer. Freelance journalists and NGO staff may have specialist media or humanitarian sector insurance. Private contractors vary widely.

## The documentation problem

In a functioning state, a death certificate is issued by a municipal registry, a forensic examination happens at a government facility, and a health ministry issues export clearance. In Libya:

- Death certificates are issued by whichever authority controls the relevant municipality
- Forensic capacity is extremely limited and varies by location
- Export clearance requires negotiation with the controlling authority — which may include unofficial fees
- Documents are in Arabic and require certified English translation

This is not a document checklist situation. It is a negotiation with armed actors, and it requires people who understand those actors and speak the relevant dialects.

## What to do when someone dies in Libya

1. Call the FCDO Emergency Travel Line immediately: 020 7008 5000 (available 24 hours)
2. Do not attempt to enter Libya to assist — UK consular protection is unavailable in-country
3. Contact British Embassy Tunis to initiate formal registration
4. Engage a UK repatriation specialist with verified Libya experience before taking any other step
5. Notify the employer or insurer — most Libya cases are insurer-led
