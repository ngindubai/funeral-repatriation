Bosnia and Herzegovina is a mid-tier Balkan repatriation destination. There is a resident British Embassy in Sarajevo, functioning civil documentation institutions, and established cargo connections from Sarajevo Airport. The main variable is the administrative entity in which the death occurred.

The country operates as two entities with parallel systems: the Federation of Bosnia and Herzegovina (covering Sarajevo, Mostar, and most of the centre and west) and Republika Srpska (covering Banja Luka and eastern regions). Each has its own forensic medicine institute and administrative chain.

## The consular process

The British Embassy Sarajevo is the primary consular contact for British nationals dying in Bosnia and Herzegovina. The Embassy handles death registration, issues notifications for UK probate purposes, and liaises with local authorities.

The Embassy is experienced with repatriation coordination — Sarajevo is an established expat and tourist destination, and Mostar attracts significant UK visitor numbers particularly in summer.

For families in the UK, the starting point is the FCDO Emergency Travel Line: 020 7008 5000.

## Federation versus Republika Srpska

The entity in which the death occurs determines the administrative path:

**Federation of Bosnia and Herzegovina (Sarajevo, Mostar, Travnik, Tuzla area):**
- Death registered through the municipal vital records office (Matičar) in the relevant municipality
- Non-natural deaths referred to the Federal Institute of Forensic Medicine, Sarajevo
- Export permit issued by the cantonal or Federal health ministry

**Republika Srpska (Banja Luka, Trebinje, East Sarajevo):**
- Death registered through the municipality vital records office (Matičar) in Republika Srpska
- Non-natural deaths referred to the Institute of Forensic Medicine, Banja Luka
- Export permit issued by Republika Srpska health ministry

Cross-entity cases — where, for example, a British tourist dies near an entity boundary — can involve a brief administrative coordination step between the two systems. This is unusual but not uncommon in the border areas around Sarajevo.

## Mostar: the UK tourist factor

Mostar is the most visited UK tourist destination in Bosnia and Herzegovina. The Old Bridge (Stari Most) and the old city attract significant summer visitor numbers. Tour operators including G Adventures, Exodus, and specialist Balkans firms include Mostar as a standard stop.

Deaths in Mostar are handled through the Federation system — the municipal vital records office in Mostar and, for non-natural deaths, the Federal Institute in Sarajevo. Mostar Airport (OMO) has limited scheduled service; cargo typically routes via Sarajevo.

Common causes of death in tourist areas:
- Road traffic accidents on mountain roads
- Heart attacks during summer heat
- Hiking incidents in Herzegovina and the Sutjeska National Park area

## Documentation and language

Official documents are in Bosnian, Croatian, or Serbian using Latin script (the Federation primarily uses Latin script; Republika Srpska may produce documents in both Latin and Cyrillic). UK authorities require certified English translations of all documents.

Core document set:

| Document | Issuer |
|---|---|
| Death certificate (Smrtovnica) | Municipal vital records office |
| Forensic institute certificate | Federal or RS Institute of Forensic Medicine |
| Police report and clearance | Local police / MoI |
| Embalming certificate | Licensed funeral director |
| Export permit | Cantonal / entity health ministry |
| British Embassy consular registration | British Embassy Sarajevo |

## The landmine legacy

Bosnia and Herzegovina's rural and mountainous areas still contain landmines and unexploded ordnance from the 1992-1995 war. The Mine Action Centre of Bosnia and Herzegovina (BHMAC) maintains cleared area maps. UK visitors in rural areas should keep to established paths.

Any death occurring in a suspected mined area may involve additional police and BHMAC protocols before the body can be recovered. Families should be aware this is a possible complication in rural hiking or adventure tourism cases.

## What to do first

Call the FCDO Emergency Travel Line: 020 7008 5000. Then contact the British Embassy Sarajevo: +387 33 282 200.

Engage a specialist repatriation firm with Balkan experience. Bosnia and Herzegovina's dual administrative structure requires familiarity that a general funeral director is unlikely to have.
