## Repatriation from Switzerland: What Families Need to Know

Switzerland is a low-complexity European repatriation by most measures. It is a Strasbourg Convention signatory, has direct UK flights from two major airports, and the forensic medicine institutes at Zurich, Lausanne, and Bern operate efficiently. For deaths in cities — Zurich, Geneva, Basel, Bern — the process is fast.

The challenge is the Alpine dimension. Switzerland attracts enormous numbers of British winter tourists to ski resorts in Valais (Verbier, Zermatt, Saas-Fee), Graubuenden (Davos, Klosters), and the Bernese Oberland (Grindelwald, Wengen, Murren). Skiing, off-piste accidents, and altitude-related illness create a steady pattern of British deaths at altitude. Mountain rescue recovery must precede all documentation.

### Cantonal Administration: The Key to Understanding Switzerland

Switzerland is federal to a degree that affects repatriation directly. There is no single national authority for death administration. Each of the 26 cantons runs its own civil registry (Zivilstandsamt / Etat civil) and cantonal health department. The Leichentransportbewilligung — the international transport permit — is issued by the cantonal medical officer (Kantonsarzt), not by a federal body.

This means that the authority responsible for issuing the transport permit for a death in Verbier (Valais canton) is the cantonal department in Sion. For a death in Davos (Graubuenden canton), it is the cantonal authority in Chur. For a death in Zurich, it is the Zurich cantonal health department.

The British Embassy Bern coordinates consular assistance across all cantons.

### The Documentation Chain

1. Todesschein (death certificate) from the cantonal Zivilstandsamt.
2. Staatsanwaltschaft release order (for unnatural deaths — cantonal public prosecution).
3. Post-mortem report from the Institut fuer Rechtsmedizin (IRM) if required.
4. Leichentransportbewilligung from the Kantonsarzt (cantonal health officer).
5. Embalming certificate.

The Leichentransportbewilligung is accepted directly by UK authorities under the Strasbourg Convention. No additional consular legalisation is required.

### Alpine and Ski Resort Deaths

The Bergrettung (Swiss mountain rescue) and the Rega (Swiss air rescue) recover bodies from Alpine terrain. Once the body is handed to the local police, the cantonal Staatsanwaltschaft is notified for unnatural deaths.

Post-mortems for ski deaths are carried out at the IRM facility relevant to the canton. Zurich IRM covers much of German-speaking Switzerland. CURML at the University of Lausanne covers French-speaking Switzerland (Valais, Vaud). The University of Bern forensic institute covers Bern canton.

Mountain recovery in poor conditions — overnight, in avalanche terrain, or on remote high-altitude routes — adds time that is outside anyone's control.

### Language

Documents are in German in the German-speaking cantons (which include Zurich, Bern, Graubuenden, and most German-speaking ski resorts), French in the French-speaking cantons (Vaud, Geneva, Valais), and Italian in Ticino. Certified English translations are required for all documents used in UK entry formalities. Ensure the translator is familiar with Swiss administrative German or French — it differs from standard German or French in legal terminology.

### Routing

Zurich Airport (ZRH) is the primary hub with direct British Airways and Swiss International Air Lines connections to London Heathrow. Geneva Airport (GVA) offers direct connections to Heathrow and Gatwick. Both airports have established cargo handling for human remains.

For Alpine resort deaths in Valais, the body typically transfers from the local funeral director to Geneva before UK departure. For Graubuenden or Bern canton cases, Zurich is the natural hub.
