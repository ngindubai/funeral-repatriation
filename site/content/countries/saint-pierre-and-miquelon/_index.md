Saint Pierre and Miquelon is a French overseas collectivity comprising eight small islands off the south coast of Newfoundland, Canada. It is the last territorial remnant of France's once-vast North American presence. The total population is approximately 6,000, concentrated almost entirely on the islands of Saint-Pierre and Miquelon-Langlade. The administrative capital is Saint-Pierre.

Geographically, the islands sit 25 kilometres from the Burin Peninsula of Newfoundland, but legally and administratively they are France. French civil law applies in full. All official documentation is in French.

## Who to call first

Contact the FCDO on **020 7008 5000** (24 hours). The British Embassy in Paris (non-resident for Saint Pierre and Miquelon) is the UK consular authority. Given the proximity to Canada, the British High Commission Ottawa (+1 613 237 1530) can sometimes provide practical assistance, but the legal consular authority remains the British Embassy Paris.

## Legal framework

Death registration follows the French civil registration system. The death is declared at the mairie (town hall) in Saint-Pierre. The death certificate (acte de décès) is issued by the officier de l'état civil at the mairie.

Non-natural deaths are reported to the Gendarmerie nationale detachment on the islands. The Procureur de la République (via the Tribunal judiciaire de Saint-Pierre-et-Miquelon) decides whether an autopsy or judicial investigation is required. Forensic pathology resources are limited on the islands: complex cases may require transfer to Canada or France for examination.

## The documentation chain

**1. Acte de décès** from the mairie of Saint-Pierre (or Miquelon-Langlade, depending on commune of death).

**2. Gendarmerie clearance** for non-natural deaths.

**3. Procureur clearance** where a judicial referral is made.

**4. Autorisation de transport de corps hors de France** (international transport permit) from the Préfet of Saint Pierre and Miquelon. This is the central document authorising the body to leave.

**5. Embalming certificate** (certificat d'embaumement) — required by IATA and by French law for international transport.

**6. Freedom from infection certificate** (certificat de non-contagion).

**7. Certified French-to-English translations** of all documents for UK authorities.

**8. IATA cargo documentation.**

Source: Code général des collectivités territoriales (France); Préfecture de Saint-Pierre-et-Miquelon, 2024.

## Routing to the UK

Saint-Pierre Airport (FSP) has limited scheduled air services. Air Saint-Pierre operates flights to Halifax (YHZ, Nova Scotia) and to St John's (YYT, Newfoundland). These are the two primary connection points for international routing.

From Halifax: Air Canada and British Airways codeshare connections to London Heathrow (LHR).
From St John's: Air Canada connections to London via Halifax or Toronto.

All routing transits through Canada. Canadian transit documentation for human remains may be required depending on whether the body is in transit or being processed at a Canadian airport. The specialist repatriation firm coordinates the cargo booking and Canadian transit requirements.

North Atlantic weather is a genuine operational constraint. Severe fog and winter storms routinely disrupt Air Saint-Pierre services. Budget additional days when planning timelines in autumn through spring.

## No cremation

There are no cremation facilities on Saint Pierre and Miquelon. Full body repatriation is the only available option. Families wishing for cremation must arrange this after arrival in the UK, or consider transfer to Halifax for cremation before the trans-Atlantic leg.

## Timeline from Saint Pierre and Miquelon

- Natural death, hospital-certified: 10 to 21 days
- Gendarmerie involvement: 14 to 28 days
- Judicial investigation: 4 to 8 weeks or more
- Weather disruption: add 2 to 7 days in winter

## Practical considerations

Saint Pierre and Miquelon is a niche destination visited primarily by Canadians from Newfoundland and Quebec, and by French nationals. British tourists are rare. The most common scenario for UK families involves a British national who is resident in Canada (particularly Newfoundland) and has died while visiting the islands, or a Canadian of British origin.

Funeral services on the islands are limited. The Préfet's office and the mairie in Saint-Pierre are the key administrative contacts. The UK specialist repatriation firm should coordinate directly with the local French funeral director and the Préfet's office from the outset.
