The Cocos (Keeling) Islands are an Australian external territory in the eastern Indian Ocean, approximately 2750 kilometres northwest of Perth. The territory consists of two atolls: North Keeling (uninhabited, a national park) and the South Keeling atoll, which contains West Island, with the airport and government facilities, and Home Island, where the Cocos Malay community lives.

Australian law applies in full. Death registration, Coroner's jurisdiction, and repatriation procedures all follow Western Australia's framework. UK families dealing with a death here should contact the FCDO on 020 7008 5000 (available 24 hours) and expect consular assistance to be provided remotely through the British High Commission in Canberra.

## Legal Framework and Authorities

This is an Australian external territory. The Administrator of Cocos (Keeling) Islands is the chief representative of the Australian Government. The Australian Federal Police maintains a presence on the island. Non-natural deaths fall under the jurisdiction of the Western Australia Coroner.

The British High Commission in Canberra can be contacted for consular assistance, and an Honorary Consul operates in Perth for closer liaison. All documentation will be in English and follow Australian legal standards.

The Australian Government's Smartraveller advisory covers the Cocos (Keeling) Islands; UK families should also review [gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice/cocos-keeling-islands](https://www.gov.uk/foreign-travel-advice/cocos-keeling-islands).

## The Repatriation Route

The standard route is:

1. Transfer from Home Island to West Island (if death occurs on Home Island)
2. Flight CCK–Perth (PER) approximately 3 hours, via Virgin Australia (approximately twice weekly)
3. Perth–London Heathrow (LHR) approximately 17 hours via Qantas or partner airlines

Once in Perth, the logistics of international air repatriation are well-established. The main constraint is the twice-weekly CCK–Perth frequency.

## Community Considerations

Home Island's Cocos Malay community is predominantly Muslim. Islamic burial practice traditionally requires interment within 24 hours. If the deceased was Muslim, the family and community leaders may request local burial rather than repatriation. This is a deeply personal decision and a specialist firm can provide advice on how to proceed sensitively and practically.

West Island's population is more mixed, with Australian and non-Muslim backgrounds more common.
