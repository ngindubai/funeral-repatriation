## Repatriation from Denmark: What Families Need to Know

Denmark is a low-complexity European repatriation. It is an EU member, a Strasbourg Convention signatory, has direct UK flights from Copenhagen Kastrup, and operates an efficient forensic pathology system through the Retsmedicinsk Institut at the University of Copenhagen. For mainland Denmark deaths, the process is among the quickest in Europe.

The picture is different if the death occurred in Greenland or the Faroe Islands, both of which are part of the Kingdom of Denmark but operate under their own Home Rule administrations.

### Strasbourg Convention

Both Denmark and the UK are signatories to the Strasbourg Convention on the Transfer of Mortal Remains (1973). In practice, this means the Danish transport permit (Udrejsetilladelse), issued by the National Board of Health (Sundhedsstyrelsen), is accepted directly by UK authorities. Additional consular legalisation steps required in non-signatory countries are not needed.

### The Documentation Chain

For natural deaths, the attending physician issues the D°dsattest (death certificate) and formalities are minimal.

For unnatural, sudden, or suspicious deaths, the Rigspolitiet (National Police) investigates. The Retsmedicinsk Institut carries out post-mortems where required. Once investigation is confirmed complete and the post-mortem report (if any) is issued, Sundhedsstyrelsen issues the Udrejsetilladelse. The process is well-documented and familiar to the established Danish funeral directors who handle repatriation work.

### Copenhagen Routing

Copenhagen Airport (CPH) has direct connections to London Heathrow and Gatwick with SAS and British Airways. It is a well-connected hub with multiple daily UK departures and established cargo handling procedures.

### Greenland Deaths

Greenland is geographically vast and sparsely populated. The Greenland Police Service (Gr°nlands Politi) operates under the Naalakkersuisut (Greenlandic Government) rather than the Danish national administration. Forensic capacity is limited. Transfers to Denmark via Kangerlussuaq (SFJ) or Nuuk (GOH) are the norm before UK departure, and the logistical chain is notably more complex than any mainland case.

If a death has occurred in Greenland, engage a specialist repatriation firm with Arctic logistics experience. The timeline is typically 14 to 28 days longer than an equivalent mainland Denmark case.

### Faroe Islands Deaths

The Faroe Islands Police (L°greglan) operates under Faroese Home Rule. Deaths are processed locally before the case transfers to the Danish mainland system. Routing is via Copenhagen before UK departure. Add 5 to 10 days compared with a mainland case.
