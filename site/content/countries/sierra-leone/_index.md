Sierra Leone has a long and significant relationship with the United Kingdom. Freetown was founded as a settlement for freed slaves. The country is a Commonwealth member with English as the official language, and the British High Commission maintains a resident presence in Freetown.

There is one defining logistical feature of every Sierra Leone repatriation case: the estuary crossing.

## The Lungi Airport estuary crossing

Lungi International Airport (FNA) is not in Freetown. It is located on a peninsula across the Sierra Leone River estuary from the city. Every transfer between Freetown and the airport requires crossing the estuary by ferry (approximately 45 minutes), hovercraft, or helicopter (Paramount Aviation offers charter services).

This applies to every single repatriation case. There is no road bridge. The crossing is an unavoidable logistical step. Ferry services can be suspended by rough weather or mechanical issues. This is a factor in every cost and timeline estimate.

## Forensic and administrative process

Natural deaths in Freetown are registered at the local registrar's office. Unnatural deaths require coroner inquest and a post-mortem examination at Connaught Hospital. Connaught Hospital is the main public hospital and forensic centre in Freetown, but its forensic pathology capacity is limited. Complex post-mortems can be significantly delayed.

## Upcountry deaths

Deaths in the Northern, Eastern, and Southern provinces require road transfer to Freetown. Road conditions deteriorate from May to November during the rainy season. Deaths in Bo, Kenema, Kailahun, and other provincial centres involve significantly longer timelines.

## Routing to the UK

From Lungi Airport, routing connects via Casablanca (Royal Air Maroc) or Brussels (Brussels Airlines). There are no direct UK flights from Sierra Leone.

Cremation is not widely available in Sierra Leone. Body repatriation is the standard approach.
