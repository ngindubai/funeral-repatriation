Venezuela's economic and political crisis, accelerating since 2015, has fundamentally changed the repatriation landscape. Most major international airlines have suspended Venezuelan routes. Healthcare infrastructure faces chronic shortages of medicines, equipment, and basic supplies. Administrative processes that should take days routinely take weeks.

The British Embassy is resident in Caracas, operating with reduced staffing.

## Who dies in Venezuela?

British tourist numbers to Venezuela have fallen dramatically since 2015. The majority of British nationals who die in Venezuela now are expats or oil industry workers, particularly in the Maracaibo Basin (Venezuela's traditional oil heartland) and the Orinoco Belt. A smaller number of adventure travellers visit Canaima National Park (Angel Falls) and the tepui formations in Bolivar state.

## The administrative process

Natural deaths are registered with the Registro Civil. Unnatural deaths are investigated by the CICPC (Cuerpo de Investigaciones Cientificas, Penales y Criminalisticas), Venezuela's forensic and criminal investigation body. Civil registry documentation goes through SAREN. The Ministry of Health (MPPS) issues the export permit.

Each of these institutions operates within Venezuela's degraded administrative environment. Delays at any stage are common.

Embalming capacity can be affected by chronic supply shortages. Confirm availability of materials with your local funeral home before proceeding.

## Air connections

Simon Bolivar International Airport (CCS) at Maiquetia serves Caracas. Most major international carriers have suspended Venezuelan routes. Cargo typically routes via Bogota (Avianca/LATAM), Panama City (Copa Airlines), or Miami on the few remaining carriers. There are no direct UK flights.

## FCDO travel advice

FCDO advises against all travel to border regions near Colombia (Apure and Tachira states), Brazil (Amazonas state), and Guyana (Bolivar state). All three have elevated criminal and conflict risk.
