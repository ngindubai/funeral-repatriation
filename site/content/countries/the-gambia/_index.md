The Gambia is the smallest country on mainland Africa and one of the most popular West African package holiday destinations for UK travellers. Winter sun packages from November to April bring tens of thousands of British visitors each year. Most stay on the coastal strip between Kololi and Bakau, south of Banjul.

When a British national dies here, the process is less complicated than in most of West Africa. English is the official language. The Gambia is a Commonwealth member. There is a resident British High Commission in Banjul. Documentation is in English throughout.

The most common causes of death for British visitors are cardiac events in older tourists, road accidents on the South Bank Road, drownings, and deaths following illness at coastal hotels. Upcountry deaths, while less frequent, require road transfer to Banjul and take considerably longer.

## The Repatriation Process

Your first call should be to the British High Commission in Banjul and the FCDO Emergency Travel Line: 020 7008 5000. From there, a specialist UK repatriation company takes over the practical logistics.

Natural deaths are registered at the local civil registry. Unnatural deaths go to the coroner and require a post-mortem at Edward Francis Small Teaching Hospital (EFSTH). EFSTH is the national referral hospital in Banjul and is the centre for forensic pathology. Its capacity is limited, and complex cases take time.

Once the death certificate and, where required, the coroner's release order are obtained, an export permit is issued by the Ministry of Health. The body is then embalmed to IATA standards at one of Banjul's approved funeral homes and consigned as cargo via Banjul International Airport (BJL), also known as Yundum.

Banjul receives direct UK charter flights from Manchester, Birmingham, Gatwick, and Bristol during the peak tourist season. Outside peak season, some cases route via Dakar (DSS) on the connecting Senegalese network.

## Upcountry Deaths

Deaths in the Central River Region, Upper River Region, or North Bank Region involve slow road transfer to Banjul. The Gambia River ferry crossing adds further time for some North Bank cases. Families should allow 4 to 6 weeks for upcountry cases.

## A Note on Cremation

The Gambia is a Muslim-majority country. Cremation is not widely available and is not compatible with Islamic burial practice. UK families who expected cremation should not assume it will be possible. A specialist repatriation company can advise on what is available in practice.
