India has some of the most document-intensive repatriation requirements of any country British families encounter. Bangalore adds a further layer: as the capital of Karnataka state, the documentation runs through Karnataka-specific authorities as well as central government and consular channels. Every document in the sequence is mandatory. None can be waived.

## The full documentation requirement for Bangalore

**1. Police First Information Report (FIR).** Under Indian law, all sudden, unattended, accidental, or unexplained deaths must be reported to the local police station with jurisdiction over the location of death. A First Information Report (FIR) is filed. This is not optional. Without an FIR, subsequent steps cannot proceed.

**2. Post-mortem at a government hospital.** For deaths referred following an FIR, the police order a post-mortem at a government hospital. In Bangalore, this is conducted at Victoria Hospital or Bowring and Lady Curzon Hospital, both operated by the Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike (BBMP). Private hospital post-mortems are not accepted for official repatriation purposes.

**3. Death certificate from the BBMP or Karnataka authorities.** Following the post-mortem, the death certificate is issued by the local civic authority — for Bangalore city, this is the Bruhat Bengaluru Mahanagara Palike (BBMP). Processing: 5 to 15 working days from post-mortem completion.

**4. Police No Objection Certificate (NOC).** The police station that filed the FIR issues a No Objection Certificate stating the case is closed and there is no objection to the body being repatriated. This document must accompany the remains.

**5. Embalming certificate.** Prepared by the licensed funeral director. Indian airlines and DGCA regulations require embalming for international air transport of human remains.

**6. Freedom from infection certificate.** Issued by the relevant health authority.

**7. British Deputy High Commission Bangalore attestation.** The British Deputy High Commission in Bangalore (Rachna Building, 3rd Floor, 11 Museum Road) can attest documents and register the death of a British national. Their involvement is not legally mandatory for the Indian side of the process, but many airlines and UK receiving funeral directors require DHC documentation as standard practice.

**8. Airline cargo booking and IATA documentation.** The licensed funeral director prepares all IATA-compliant cargo documentation for the Kempegowda International Airport cargo terminal.

Source: Karnataka Registration and Stamps Department, *Death Registration*, 2024; BBMP Bangalore, *Vital Records*, 2024; British Deputy High Commission Bangalore, 2024.

## Kempegowda International Airport cargo

Kempegowda International Airport Bengaluru (BLR) operates international cargo services. British Airways operates BLR-LHR direct. Emirates, Qatar Airways, and Singapore Airlines also carry cargo from BLR with UK connections via Dubai, Doha, and Singapore respectively.

## Timeline from Bangalore

- Natural death with FIR, post-mortem, and clean NOC: 14 to 28 days
- Extended police investigation or delayed post-mortem scheduling: 4 to 8 weeks
- Deaths occurring over Indian public holidays (Diwali, Republic Day, Independence Day): add 5 to 10 working days

## Key local considerations

India's bureaucratic system requires patience. Attempting to expedite any step through informal channels is strongly inadvisable and can create complications for the official record. The British Deputy High Commission in Bangalore can provide guidance and, in urgent cases, communicate with Indian authorities through official channels — but they cannot override Indian legal requirements.

Bangalore's large IT industry means deaths among British nationals working in the technology sector are among the cases handled in the city. Workplace deaths require additional documentation from the employer and may involve Karnataka's Labour Department, extending the timeline.
