## Repatriation from Belgium: What Families Need to Know

Belgium is a low-complexity European repatriation. It is an EU member state, a Strasbourg Convention signatory, and Brussels Airport has strong direct flight connectivity to London and other UK cities. The documentation process is efficient by European standards.

The main practical consideration is language. Belgium has three official language communities — Dutch/Flemish in Flanders (Antwerp, Ghent, Bruges), French in Wallonia, and a small German-speaking area in the east. Brussels is officially bilingual. Documents are issued in the language of the region where death occurred. All require certified English translation for UK entry formalities.

### The British Community in Belgium

Brussels hosts a substantial and well-established British community, concentrated around EU institutions, NATO headquarters (SHAPE at Mons), and multinational corporate headquarters. British nationals at SHAPE and NATO who die in service are handled through military channels. This page covers civilian repatriation.

British tourists visit Brussels, Bruges, Ghent, and the Ardennes. The Ardennes see British outdoor activity tourism: cycling, kayaking, hiking. These visits generate a small but consistent pattern of outdoor accident deaths.

### Commune-Level Administration

Unlike France or the UK, which have centralised or regional death administration, Belgium processes death registration and transport permits at commune level. The commune civil registry (Service de l'Etat civil / Dienst Burgerlijke Stand) issues the death certificate. The commune also issues the international transport permit.

For unnatural or sudden deaths, the Parquet (Public Prosecution) at the judicial district level must authorise body release. Forensic post-mortems are conducted at university hospital departments: UZ Leuven, CHU Liege, UZ Gent, and ULB (Universite Libre de Bruxelles) in Brussels.

### The Documentation Chain

1. Overlijdensakte / Acte de deces (death certificate) from the commune.
2. Parquet release order (for unnatural deaths).
3. Post-mortem report from the university hospital forensic department if required.
4. International transport permit (autorisation de transport de corps / vervoervergunning) from the commune.
5. Embalming certificate.

The transport permit is accepted directly by UK authorities under the Strasbourg Convention. Additional consular legalisation is not required.

### Routing

Brussels Airport (BRU) at Zaventem is the primary international hub. Multiple carriers fly direct to London Heathrow and Gatwick, including Brussels Airlines, British Airways, and easyJet. Cargo handling for human remains is well-established.

Brussels South Charleroi Airport (CRL) is used by Ryanair but has more limited cargo options for human remains. For deaths in the Liege area, Liege Airport (LGG) offers some cargo connectivity but Brussels is usually the better routing choice.
