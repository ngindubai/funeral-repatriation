Vatican City is the smallest sovereign state in the world: 0.44 square kilometres, entirely enclosed within Rome, home to a permanent population of a few hundred people. Deaths of non-Vatican citizens within its walls are rare. They do happen, usually involving a visiting pilgrim, tourist, or journalist, and each one is treated as a specialist case by the firms who handle it.

## Two embassies, one city

Here's the detail that trips people up: the British Embassy to the Holy See is a separate mission from the British Embassy to Italy, even though both are based in Rome. Contacting the wrong one wastes time on a case that's already unusual. Call the FCDO first, on 020 7008 5000, available 24 hours, and they will direct you to the correct mission.

## Who runs the civil process

Civil registration inside Vatican City is handled by the Governorate of Vatican City State, the Governatorato dello Stato della Citta del Vaticano. Death certificates are issued in Italian. Where a death isn't natural, the Gendarmerie Corps of Vatican City State, the Vatican's own police force, carries out the investigation.

If the person who died was clergy, a member of a religious order, or a Vatican employee, the relevant congregation or dicastery within the Holy See may have its own requirements running alongside the Governorate's civil process. This needs to be established early, not discovered partway through.

## No airport, no cremation

Vatican City has no airport of its own. Every repatriation routes through Rome Fiumicino Leonardo da Vinci International Airport, about 30 minutes away by road, once Italian transit law applies to the remains. And there's no cremation within Vatican City at all: the Roman Catholic Church's historical preference for burial means this option simply doesn't exist locally, so it has to be discussed with the British Embassy and a specialist firm from the outset if it matters to the family.

## Why documentation still needs care

Everything comes out in Italian, and certified English translation is required regardless of how small the case feels. Vatican City is not a Hague Apostille Convention member, so standard legalisation applies rather than the simpler apostille process used for most of its Italian neighbours.
