Djibouti sits at the southern mouth of the Red Sea, a small country that punches well above its size in strategic importance and well below it in diplomatic infrastructure. There's no resident British Embassy here.

## Where consular support actually comes from

The British Embassy Addis Ababa, in neighbouring Ethiopia, holds non-resident accreditation for Djibouti and handles death registration and the notifications a UK case needs. Everything runs remotely. That adds a coordination layer that resident-embassy countries simply don't have. Call the FCDO Emergency Travel Line first, on +44 (0)20 7008 5000, and expect the Addis Ababa office to take the lead from there.

## The état civil system

Djibouti's civil registration runs through the état civil, a structure inherited from French colonial administration. The attending physician issues a medical certificate of cause of death, and the registrar produces the acte de décès in French and Arabic. None of it is usable for UK purposes until a certified translator has been through it line by line.

## When the Tribunal gets involved

Violent, sudden, or unexplained deaths go to the Tribunal de Première Instance. The Police Nationale attend the scene, an examining magistrate reviews the file, and only then does an authorisation for release follow. For a straightforward accident case, this step alone typically takes ten to twenty-one days.

## Getting home

Djibouti-Ambouli International Airport connects to Addis Ababa, Dubai, and Istanbul. Ethiopian Airlines via Addis Ababa is the most established route for UK-bound cargo, not least because it's the same city where the accrediting embassy sits.
