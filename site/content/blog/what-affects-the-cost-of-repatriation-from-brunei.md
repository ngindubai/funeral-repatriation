Brunei is one of the more manageable repatriation cases in the region, with a resident British High Commission and a direct flight to London. The one factor that genuinely changes how a case runs isn't geography. It's religious classification.

## Two tracks, depending on faith

Islam is Brunei's state religion, and Muslim deaths fall under the Department of Religious Affairs, known locally as JHEAS, which must issue clearance before an export permit can be granted. That typically takes two to five working days and runs alongside civil registration, not instead of it. Non-Muslim deaths skip this step entirely and follow civil registration alone, with the death certificate issued in Malay and English by the civil registry.

## Non-natural deaths add time either way

Whatever the religious classification, an accident or a sudden illness without a prior medical history brings in the Royal Brunei Police Force. The Attorney General's Chambers may then require an inquest before the death certificate can be issued, adding roughly seven to fourteen days on top of the standard process. This applies regardless of faith.

## The direct flight is a genuine advantage

Royal Brunei Airlines flies directly between Brunei International Airport and London Heathrow. That removes the connecting leg, most commonly through Singapore, that many regional cases would otherwise need, along with the extra cost and handling that comes with it.

## A separate process for military cases

British Garrison Brunei, home to the 1st Battalion, Royal Gurkha Rifles and associated units, is one of the UK's longest-standing overseas deployments. A death among Garrison personnel or dependants follows a distinct route through the Ministry of Defence's Defence Inquiries and a dedicated casualty notification officer, not the civilian process described here.
