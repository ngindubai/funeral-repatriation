We describe Bosnia and Herzegovina as a manageable mid-range Balkan case, and it genuinely is, with a resident British Embassy and working civil institutions. Two things still make one case cost more than another.

## One country, two administrative systems

Bosnia and Herzegovina runs as two entities in parallel: the Federation of Bosnia and Herzegovina, covering Sarajevo, Mostar, and most of the centre and west, and Republika Srpska, covering Banja Luka and the eastern regions. Each has its own forensic medicine institute and its own administrative chain. Which one applies to your case depends entirely on where the death occurred, and occasionally, near an entity boundary, a brief coordination step between the two systems is needed.

## Mostar, and what draws UK visitors there

Mostar is the most visited destination in the country for British tourists, largely thanks to the Old Bridge and the old city, and several UK tour operators run it as a standard stop on Balkans itineraries. A death there is handled through the Federation system: registered locally, referred to the Federal Institute in Sarajevo if the death wasn't straightforward.

## The landmine legacy, still relevant today

Rural and mountainous parts of the country still contain landmines and unexploded ordnance from the 1992 to 1995 war. The Mine Action Centre of Bosnia and Herzegovina maintains maps of cleared areas, but a death in or near a suspected mined zone can trigger additional police and Mine Action Centre protocols before recovery is even possible. It's not a common scenario, but it's a real one for hiking or adventure tourism cases in Herzegovina.

## Documents, scripts, and translation

Official documents appear in Bosnian, Croatian, or Serbian. Federation paperwork uses Latin script; Republika Srpska documents can appear in Latin or Cyrillic. Whichever applies, UK authorities need a certified English translation before they'll accept it.
