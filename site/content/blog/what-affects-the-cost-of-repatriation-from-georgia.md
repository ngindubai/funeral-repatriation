Georgia is a mid-complexity corridor, and the single biggest cost variable isn't distance or bureaucracy. It's finding the right translator, fast enough.

## The Mkhedruli script problem

Georgian documentation is written in Mkhedruli, a script unique to Georgia and unrelated to Cyrillic or Latin. Most translation agencies simply don't carry an accredited Georgian legal translator, and the ones who are qualified need to be recognised specifically by the British Embassy Tbilisi for death registration purposes. That's a narrow field. Waiting until documents are issued before starting this search adds a real delay on top of everything else, so it's the first thing we chase down in any Georgia case, in parallel with the Civil Registry Agency process, not after it.

## Kazbegi and Svaneti aren't the same problem

Georgia's Caucasus Mountains draw growing numbers of British trekkers, and mountain deaths follow very different cost profiles depending on where they happen. Kazbegi, around three hours from Tbilisi, has an active, well-organised mountain rescue service, and recovery from the main trails near Mount Kazbek typically takes 24 to 48 hours. Svaneti is a different matter. Reached by a long mountain road from Zugdidi, its high backcountry toward Ushba or the Russian border can involve recovery operations of 72 hours or more, and considerably longer in winter conditions.

## Forensic capacity is centralised

Georgia's National Forensics Bureau, named for Levan Samkharauli, handles post-mortems for the whole country from Tbilisi. It's a well-functioning institution and turnaround for a straightforward case is five to ten working days. What extends the timeline, and the cost, is location: getting a body from a mountain area to Tbilisi in the first place.

## Direct flights keep the last leg simple

At least the final stage is straightforward. Wizz Air runs direct services from London Gatwick to Tbilisi and also serves Kutaisi from the UK, with Turkish Airlines via Istanbul as a fallback. Confirming the carrier's human remains requirements ahead of time avoids a last-minute booking problem.

Georgia is not an EU member and has not signed the Strasbourg Convention, so the full documentation chain applies here rather than a shortened one.
