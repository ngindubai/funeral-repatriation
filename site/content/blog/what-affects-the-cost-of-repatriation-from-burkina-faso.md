Burkina Faso sits among the most demanding repatriation cases this site handles, and it is worth being direct about that from the outset. A 2022 coup, an active Sahel-linked insurgency, and the absence of any resident British diplomat combine to make even a straightforward natural death in Ouagadougou a genuinely involved process.

## Governed by a junta since 2022

Captain Ibrahim Traore's MPSR took power in September 2022, and the Conseil National pour la Sauvegarde de la Patrie has run the country's institutions since. Administrative processes reflect the priorities of a military government rather than a settled civilian bureaucracy, and that unpredictability is worth planning around rather than hoping to avoid.

## No resident Embassy, and a hard line on travel

British consular coverage for Burkina Faso comes from Accra, Ghana, not from anywhere inside the country. The FCDO advises against all travel to the northern, eastern, and western border regions and against all but essential travel elsewhere, Ouagadougou included. Where a death occurs matters enormously here. A case in the capital is difficult but workable. A case near the Mali or Niger border is not achievable through civilian channels at all.

## Two authorities, not one

French civil law governs registration through the etat civil, but that is only the first layer. Any death that is not straightforwardly natural brings in the Tribunal de Grande Instance, which has to authorise release before the death certificate can be issued, and the Police Nationale has to attend separately. Neither step substitutes for the other.

## Getting the case out

Ethiopian Airlines' route through Addis Ababa is the option most families end up relying on for UK-bound cargo. Air France also flies to Ouagadougou, though the schedule has been irregular enough that we would never book cargo around it without checking first. Royal Air Maroc via Casablanca and Brussels Airlines are the other realistic alternatives.
