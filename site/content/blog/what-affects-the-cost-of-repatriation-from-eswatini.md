Eswatini is a small kingdom, and most of what determines the final bill for a repatriation comes down to two things: where the death happened, and whether the police get involved.

## The Johannesburg leg

There's no way around it. Every repatriation from Eswatini connects through Johannesburg O.R. Tambo before it reaches London Heathrow, since no airline flies the route direct. That's an extra transit fee on top of the main air cargo charge. The saving grace is frequency: JNB to Heathrow runs multiple times a day, so the connection itself rarely becomes the bottleneck. It's a fixed cost of this corridor rather than a variable one.

## Police clearance is the real variable

A natural death with clear cause moves fast. Anything else brings in the Royal Eswatini Police Service, and that's where timelines, and cost, start to spread. Routine accident investigations clear in five to ten working days. A case flagged for criminal investigation has no fixed end date, and every extra day means more mortuary storage and more coordination work on the ground.

## One structural quirk worth knowing

Eswatini is the last absolute monarchy in sub-Saharan Africa. King Mswati III holds executive, legislative, and judicial authority, and that has a very practical consequence: there's no independent courts process to push back against an administrative delay. Firms that already have working relationships with the Registrar General's Office and local funeral directors tend to move cases faster than firms starting from scratch. That relationship, not paperwork alone, is often what keeps a case on schedule.

## Where the language barrier doesn't apply

At least documentation isn't the obstacle it is in much of the region. English is an official language, so the death certificate, medical certificate, and police clearance are all issued in English. No certified translation, no translator search, no waiting on that step.
