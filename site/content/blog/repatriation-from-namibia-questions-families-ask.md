If you're dealing with a death in Namibia, there's a fair chance a road accident is part of the story. It's worth knowing that upfront, because it shapes almost everything about how the next few weeks go.

## Why self-drive safaris carry real risk

Namibia draws British visitors for exactly the reason it's also hard going: it's vast, remote, and strikingly beautiful in ways that pull people off the main roads. Self-drive tourists commonly cover thousands of kilometres linking Etosha National Park, Sossusvlei, Damaraland, and the Skeleton Coast. Much of that driving happens on gravel roads, far from any town, with no mobile signal. A single-vehicle accident in the Kunene region or along the Skeleton Coast can take hours for emergency services simply to reach.

## What an unnatural death means for the timeline

Any death that isn't clearly natural, and that includes road accidents, triggers a State pathologist examination and a Magistrate's Court coroner process before the body can be released. Windhoek's State pathologist capacity is limited, so a complex post-mortem can take a while to complete. It's frustrating when you're waiting, but it's not something a repatriation firm can shortcut.

## The remote regions

Some parts of Namibia are genuinely hard to reach even without an emergency. The Kaokoveld, in the northwest, is accessible mainly by 4WD on rough tracks or small charter aircraft, and transfers to Windhoek from there can take several days. The Skeleton Coast Wilderness requires a special permit and its nearest airstrip, Möwe Bay, is still a full day from Windhoek by air. The Caprivi Strip, now called the Zambezi Region, borders four countries and needs transfer through Katima Mulilo first. Even Etosha, the most visited park of all, requires a stop in Tsumeb or Ondangwa before Windhoek.

## Getting home

Hosea Kutako International Airport, near Windhoek, has no direct UK flights. Everything routes through Johannesburg O.R. Tambo on British Airways, South African Airways cargo, or Ethiopian Airlines. A resident British High Commission in Windhoek and English as the sole official language both take real friction out of an already difficult situation.
