The Gambia is one of the more manageable West African corridors, English documentation and a resident British High Commission both help, but two factors still swing the cost: when in the year the death happens, and where in the country.

## Charter season versus the rest of the year

During the UK winter sun season, November through April, Banjul receives direct charter flights from Manchester, Birmingham, Gatwick, and Bristol, the same routes that bring in the tourists in the first place. That seasonal capacity keeps costs down for cases falling in that window. Outside it, routing often shifts via Dakar in Senegal instead, adding a connection and generally pushing the cost up. It's worth knowing which season a case falls into early on.

## The coast is fast, upcountry is not

Most British deaths happen along the coastal strip between Kololi and Bakau, and these cases move relatively quickly: natural death in a coastal hotel can complete in 10 to 14 days. Move away from the coast, into the Central River, Upper River, or North Bank Region, and the picture changes. Roads are slow, and North Bank cases sometimes need a Gambia River ferry crossing on top of that. Families in this situation should plan for 4 to 6 weeks, not two.

## When the coroner is involved

Natural deaths register through the local civil registry without much friction. Unnatural deaths go to the coroner and require a post-mortem at Edward Francis Small Teaching Hospital, EFSTH, in Banjul, the country's forensic referral centre. Its capacity is limited, so a case requiring detailed investigation can extend well beyond the standard timeline, with cold storage and coordination costs building accordingly.

## Cremation isn't the fallback some families expect

As a Muslim-majority country, cremation isn't standard in The Gambia and isn't compatible with Islamic burial custom. A family assuming it as a cheaper alternative to full repatriation should check with a specialist early, rather than discover the limitation partway through.
