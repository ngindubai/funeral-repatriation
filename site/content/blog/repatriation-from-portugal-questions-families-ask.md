Portugal sees a steady number of British deaths each year, driven mainly by the large retired and expat community in the Algarve and by tourism. The established British presence means the local repatriation process is well practised. This guide answers the questions families ask most.

For the full process and embassy detail, see our [complete guide to repatriation from Portugal](/blog/repatriation-from-portugal-guide/). This article focuses on the immediate questions.

## First steps

As with most cases, the priority is to find any travel insurance, because a policy often covers repatriation in full and appoints a provider. The death is registered at the local civil registry, which issues the certidao de obito. A Portuguese funeral director then carries out the local steps under the coordinator's direction.

Where there is no insurance, the family appoints a coordinator directly and the cost falls to the estate or the family.

## Why the Algarve tends to be smoother

Most British deaths in Portugal happen in the Algarve, where the expat community runs into the tens of thousands. Funeral directors around Faro, Albufeira, and Lagos handle UK repatriations as routine work. That experience shows in the paperwork and the logistics, and it usually means fewer surprises than in regions where British cases are uncommon.

Lisbon and Porto are also well served, with direct air cargo capacity to the UK.

## Timelines and post-mortems

An expected death moves through registration and documentation in 7 to 14 days in most cases. A sudden death goes to the Ministerio Publico, and a forensic examination may be ordered before release. The family cannot decline a prosecutor-ordered examination, and the body is not released until it is complete.

A local funeral is possible for families who prefer it, though most repatriate so the service can be held at home.
