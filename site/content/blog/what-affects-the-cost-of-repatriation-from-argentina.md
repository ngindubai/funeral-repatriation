Argentina is not a signatory to the Strasbourg Agreement, and that single legal fact shapes almost everything about how a case there is priced. Repatriation runs through Argentine federal and provincial law instead, with the judiciary, the forensic medical corps, and local health authorities all potentially involved depending on the circumstances of the death.

## The judicial release requirement

For a natural death, a doctor confirms cause of death and the Registro Civil issues the Acta de Defuncion in the usual way. For anything sudden, violent, or unexplained, the Poder Judicial takes jurisdiction, and a judge orders a forensic post-mortem through the Cuerpo Medico Forense. Nothing, including embalming, can proceed until that judge formally authorises release of the body. Buenos Aires processes this comparatively fast. Outside the capital, particularly in the Patagonian provinces of Santa Cruz and Tierra del Fuego, provincial timelines can run considerably longer, and that time under judicial authority is the single biggest driver of cost variation between cases.

## Getting to Buenos Aires in the first place

Argentina draws British visitors well beyond the capital: hikers to the Fitz Roy massif in Patagonia, skiers and wine tourists around Mendoza, and travellers to Iguazu Falls in the northeast. None of these regions handle international air cargo directly. A death anywhere outside Buenos Aires requires transfer to Ministro Pistarini Airport before repatriation can even begin, using domestic Aerolineas Argentinas connections or, in remote parts of Patagonia, small aircraft or road transport to reach the nearest domestic airport at all.

## No direct route to the UK

There simply isn't a direct flight from Argentina to the UK. The standard routing goes via Madrid with Iberia, occasionally via Sao Paulo instead. A multi-leg cargo booking costs more than a single direct sector, and it needs careful coordination between two or three separate handling agents rather than one. This is a fixed feature of the route, not something that varies case by case.

## The translation layer

Every document Argentina produces in a repatriation case, the death certificate, any judicial order, the forensic report, comes out in Spanish. UK authorities won't accept any of it without certified English translation, so a sworn translator is a standard cost on every single case here, whatever else is or isn't involved.
