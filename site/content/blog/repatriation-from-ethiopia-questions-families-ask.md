Ethiopia sits high on the list of places British travellers go for genuine adventure: the rock-hewn churches of Lalibela, the Simien Mountains, the Omo Valley's tribal regions, the Blue Nile falls. It's also a country where the honest answer to "how long will this take" depends enormously on exactly where the death occurred.

## Addis Ababa is where everything has to happen

Ethiopia isn't a signatory to the Strasbourg Agreement, so repatriation runs through domestic Ethiopian law and bilateral consular arrangements rather than a standardised international framework. A forensic post-mortem, where one is needed, can only take place in Addis Ababa, typically at St. Paul's Hospital Millennium Medical College. That single constraint drives almost everything else about timing.

## Federal Police jurisdiction

For a violent, sudden, or unexplained death, the Federal Police take over the case and a post-mortem may follow. Expect this to add 7 to 21 days to a natural-death baseline of 14 to 21 days in Addis Ababa itself.

## When the death happens outside Addis

This is where cases get genuinely difficult. Lalibela, the Simien Mountains, the Omo Valley: all remarkable places to visit, all a considerable distance from the capital's forensic and administrative infrastructure. The body has to physically reach Addis Ababa before the Federal Police process can even begin. Options include a charter flight from a regional airstrip, the fastest but most expensive route, a scheduled Ethiopian Airlines domestic service, or road transport for the most remote locations. Whichever is used, this transfer typically adds 2 to 5 days before official processing starts, and your specialist coordinates it directly with local agents on the ground.

## Amharic, and why it's not a quick translation job

Every document, the death certificate, the Federal Police release, the export permit, comes out in Amharic, written in the Ge'ez script. This isn't a widely available translation service; specialist Amharic-English translators are a genuinely narrow pool, and this step alone typically adds 5 to 10 days regardless of how quickly everything else moves.

## Contacting the Embassy

The British Embassy in Addis Ababa is on Comoros Street. Call the FCDO Emergency Travel Line first, 020 7008 5000, available at any hour, then the Embassy directly on +251 11 617 0100. The Embassy also covers Djibouti, so make clear you're calling about an Ethiopian case.

## Getting home

Once the export permit is issued, Ethiopian Airlines' major hub at Addis Ababa Bole Airport offers direct and one-stop cargo services to London Heathrow, genuinely one of the more efficient final legs anywhere on the African continent.
