France is unusual among the countries on this site for one simple reason: it's close enough that the road is sometimes genuinely the better option, not just a fallback.

## Road versus air, and why it isn't automatic

For deaths in northern France, a hearse can collect the deceased and cross to the UK by ferry or the Eurotunnel, using a sealed coffin and the laissez-passer mortuaire. This route can be quicker and less costly than air cargo, and it's one of the clearest cost levers available on any corridor we handle. It only applies within reach of the crossing though. A death near Nice, Marseille, or Toulouse is too far south for road to make sense, and air cargo from the nearest airport becomes the practical choice instead.

## The mairie and the acte de deces

Every death in France is registered at the local mairie, the town hall, which issues the acte de deces. This is usually fast for an expected death and doesn't add meaningful cost on its own. It's what happens next that determines the timeline.

## When the procureur gets involved

A sudden, violent, or unexplained death is referred to the procureur de la Republique, the public prosecutor, who can order a medico-legal examination before release. This is a legal requirement rather than a choice, and it's the single biggest driver of both cost and delay in an otherwise straightforward country. There's no way to speed up or skip a prosecutor-ordered examination.

## Staying in the UK doesn't cost you anything

One genuine advantage in France: the family very rarely needs to travel. A French funeral director and a UK-based coordinator can manage almost the entire process at a distance. Travel only becomes relevant if the family wants to be present, if formal identification is required, or if there's property or a vehicle to sort out, and none of that holds up the repatriation itself.

If there was travel insurance in place, check the policy first: many cover the full cost of repatriation and appoint a provider directly.
