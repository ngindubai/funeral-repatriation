Seychelles has one of the best-regarded consular relationships of any Indian Ocean destination for British families, and for a straightforward death on Mahé, that shows. English documentation removes one of the most common sources of delay elsewhere in the world. Two things still catch families out more often than they expect: how forensic cases are handled, and how spread out the islands actually are.

## The forensic bottleneck

Seychelles Hospital in Victoria is the only place in the country equipped for forensic post-mortems. For a routine case, that's not a problem. But any death that's forensically ambiguous, particularly anything involving water, disputed drowning, a diving incident, an unclear current fatality, can trigger a decision to transfer the body to Réunion, about 1,600 kilometres south-west, or to South Africa for specialist examination. That decision belongs to the local authorities, not the family, and once made, it adds 10 to 20 days that can't realistically be shortened.

If your loved one's death involved water in any way, it's worth asking the question early rather than assuming a routine timeline.

## 115 islands, three that matter most

Most British visitors stay on Mahé, Praslin, or La Digue. Praslin connects to Mahé by a short flight. La Digue is different: no airport, boat access only from Praslin, and weather-dependent crossings. A death there means a boat transfer first, then a flight, before the documentation chain even starts. Private island deaths, Denis, Bird, Silhouette, Frégate, need charter boat or helicopter evacuation to Mahé instead.

## The documentation chain

Everything is in English, which helps considerably: a death certificate from the Civil Status Office, a Seychelles Police Force report for unnatural deaths, the post-mortem report (from Mahé or wherever the case was transferred to), a Ministry of Health export permit, and an embalming certificate.

## Coming home

British Airways runs a seasonal direct service from Seychelles International Airport to London Heathrow; Emirates and Air Seychelles offer connecting routes at other times of year. The British High Commission in Victoria, well practised in these cases given tourist volumes, should be your first call alongside the FCDO's 24-hour line, 020 7008 5000.
