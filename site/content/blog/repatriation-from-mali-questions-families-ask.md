In 2023, Air France suspended its service to Bamako. That single airline decision now shapes every UK-bound repatriation from Mali, forcing every case through a third country instead of a direct European connection, and it's one of several factors that make Mali one of the longer, more demanding West African corridors this service handles.

## Government by transition

Mali has been under military rule since Colonel Assimi Goïta's coup in May 2021. The Transition government controls every layer of civil administration, the Ministry of Health, civil registrars, and the Police Nationale, and administrative decisions can be delayed without explanation. There is no resident British Embassy; the British Embassy in Dakar, Senegal, provides non-resident consular coverage instead, handling death registration, next-of-kin notification, and liaison with Malian authorities remotely.

## French civil law, with a judicial step for anything unusual

Death registration runs through the état civil, the civil status office, with the attending physician certifying cause of death in French and the civil registrar issuing the acte de décès. For an accident, an act of violence, or a sudden unexplained death, the Parquet, the public prosecutor's office, has to issue judicial clearance before the death certificate is released. That step carries no guaranteed timeline under the current administration.

## A country that buries quickly

Mali is predominantly Muslim, and Islamic burial tradition favours interment within a day. If repatriation is the family's intention, specialist involvement from the first hours matters more here than almost anywhere else this service works. Local custom moves fast, and a case can be settled locally before a family in the UK has even been fully informed.

## The route home, post-2023

Bamako-Sénou International Airport now routes UK-bound cases through Addis Ababa with Ethiopian Airlines, the preferred option since Air France's 2023 withdrawal, or via Casablanca with Royal Air Maroc, with Abidjan on Air Côte d'Ivoire as a secondary alternative.

## The north is not accessible

FCDO advises against all travel to northern Mali, the regions of Kidal, Gao, and Timbuktu, where JNIM and ISWAP-affiliated armed groups hold territory. Civilian body recovery from these areas isn't achievable, and cases near the borders with Burkina Faso and Niger face the same severe constraints.
