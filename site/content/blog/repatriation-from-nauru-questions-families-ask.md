Nauru is easy to miss on a map, and that's part of the point: at twenty-one square kilometres, it's the smallest island state anywhere, and the whole coastline can be driven in about twenty minutes. It's also one of the more demanding places to manage a repatriation from, simply because there's so little infrastructure to work with.

## A single airline, a narrow window

Nauru International Airport is served exclusively by Nauru Airlines, flying to Brisbane and to Honiara or Tarawa, and frequency is limited to a handful of services a week. Cargo capacity is restricted too. This shapes the entire case: there's no alternative carrier to fall back on if timing doesn't line up, so a firm coordinating a Nauru case has to plan around the schedule that actually exists, not the one that would be convenient.

## Very little medical and mortuary capacity

Nauru's medical infrastructure is limited, and complex cases have historically been evacuated to Australia or Fiji rather than handled on the island. What embalming and holding capacity is available at any given time needs confirming directly, which is exactly the kind of thing a specialist firm should be doing before the case moves any further.

## Who handles what

Death registration goes through the island's civil registry, and Nauru Police Force investigates and clears any non-natural death. Documentation is in English, reflecting Nauru's history of close Australian administrative involvement. There's no resident British diplomatic presence: the British High Commission in Suva, Fiji, covers Nauru on a non-resident basis.

## Getting home via Brisbane

Every route to the UK goes through Brisbane. Because of that, Australian transit documentation for human remains has to be arranged alongside the standard Nauruan paperwork, since Australia has its own detailed rules for how remains move through its airports. Cremation isn't available on Nauru, so full body repatriation is the only route.

None of this reflects any political risk; the FCDO carries no significant advisory against Nauru. It's simply a function of size and isolation, and it's exactly the kind of case where a firm's Pacific experience matters.
