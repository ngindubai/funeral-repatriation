Abidjan doesn't look like the rest of the Ivory Coast story you might expect. It's a large, sophisticated West African city, home to the country's international airport, several teaching hospitals, and a genuine British diplomatic presence. Most British deaths here happen in or near the city, or in the western tourist zone around Man and the Tai National Park.

## What Abidjan gets right

A resident British Embassy means direct consular support is available from the outset, which is more than many West African destinations can offer. Natural deaths register at the local mairie, the town hall. Where something isn't natural, the case goes to the Institut National de Medecine Legale instead, with post-mortem capacity concentrated at three teaching hospitals in the city.

Everything, without exception, is documented in French. Certified translation into English is needed before UK death registration, probate, or an insurance claim can go anywhere, and that's a genuine cost and time addition compared with an English-speaking destination.

## Outside the city, things slow down fast

The interior tells a different story. Forensic capacity thins out quickly once you leave Abidjan, and a death there needs a road transfer to the capital before a post-mortem can happen at all. The northern regions near the borders with Mali and Burkina Faso, Korhogo and Odienne among them, carry elevated security concerns and very limited medical infrastructure. Reading the current FCDO travel advisory for that area is worth doing before travel, not after.

## The flight home

There's no direct UK service from Felix Houphouet-Boigny International Airport. Air France via Paris is the standard route. Brussels Airlines and Royal Air Maroc, via Brussels and Casablanca respectively, are the usual alternatives, and cargo space on any of these should be confirmed early rather than assumed.
