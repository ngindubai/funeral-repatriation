Finland ranks among the least complicated European repatriations for a UK family, and the Strasbourg Convention is the main reason why. Both countries are signatories, so the transport permit issued by Finnish authorities carries straight through UK entry formalities. There's no extra legalisation step to chase.

## The Strasbourg Convention advantage

Valvira, Finland's National Supervisory Authority for Welfare and Health, issues the Matkustuslupa, the international transport permit. Because the UK and Finland both signed the Strasbourg Convention on the Transfer of Mortal Remains, that permit is accepted directly by UK authorities. Countries outside the Convention need an additional consular legalisation stage. Finland doesn't.

## Two very different starting points

A death in Helsinki, or anywhere on Finland's efficiently administered mainland, moves through a predictable chain: a Kuolintodistus (death certificate) from the attending physician, and for unnatural or sudden deaths, an investigation by Finnish Police before Valvira issues the permit. Straightforward cases complete in around 5 days.

Lapland is different. Finland's Northern Lights tourism, centred on Rovaniemi, Saariselka, and Levi, brings a steady stream of British visitors north each winter. A death there means transfer to Oulu, the nearest city with forensic pathology capacity, several hours by road or a short flight, before the THL Forensic Medicine Unit can begin its work. Snowmobile accidents, hypothermia, and falls on ice are the most common causes in this region.

## Language and translation

All documentation is issued in Finnish, with some bilingual coastal regions also producing Swedish paperwork. A certified English translation is required before UK authorities will act on any of it. Your repatriation coordinator arranges this as a standard part of the process, not an extra step to chase separately.

## Routing home

Helsinki-Vantaa Airport has direct connections to London Heathrow and Gatwick, operated by Finnair and British Airways. Cargo handling here is well-established, with clear procedures for human remains. For Lapland cases, the domestic transfer to Helsinki adds to the total timeline but doesn't complicate the routing itself once the body reaches the capital.
