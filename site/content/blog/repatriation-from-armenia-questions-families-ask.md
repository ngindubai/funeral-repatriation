Yerevan has quietly become a destination for cultural and short-break travel from the UK, and the British Embassy there is experienced with consular death cases. What catches most families off guard isn't the process itself. It's the script.

## The script, not the paperwork, is the bottleneck

Armenian is written in its own alphabet, one with no relation to Cyrillic, Georgian, or Arabic. Every document that matters, the death certificate, the police record, the post-mortem report, the Ministry of Health export permit, is issued in it. Certified translation into English is required before UK purposes can be met, and the pool of UK-accredited Armenian legal translators is genuinely small. Contact the British Embassy Yerevan as soon as death is notified and ask for their current recommended translators before documents are even issued. Someone lined up in advance can start the moment paperwork is ready. Someone found afterward adds a week or more that didn't need to happen.

## Forensic processing runs through one facility

Every unnatural, unexplained, or third-party death in Armenia goes through the Republican Institute of Forensic Medical Expertise in Yerevan. It's the country's only national forensic facility, and regional cases are transferred there rather than examined locally. This centralisation is efficient for the institute but adds a transfer step for anyone who dies outside the capital.

## Aragats and Sevan carry their own delays

Mount Aragats, the country's highest point at 4,090 metres, draws serious trekkers, and a death there needs rescue and evacuation before anything else can happen. Lake Sevan, the largest lake in the Caucasus and a popular tourist spot roughly an hour from Yerevan, is where a number of drowning incidents occur. Deaths near the Debed Canyon or the border regions require road transfer into Yerevan; the Turkish border is largely closed and the Azerbaijan border is conflict-sensitive, so neither offers a shortcut.

## Getting home: no direct cargo route

There's no direct cargo flight between Armenia and the UK. The standard connections run via Vienna with Austrian Airlines or via Frankfurt with Lufthansa. Wizz Air's direct Luton to Yerevan service is passenger-only and doesn't carry human remains, so it isn't an option here even though it looks like the obvious route on paper.
