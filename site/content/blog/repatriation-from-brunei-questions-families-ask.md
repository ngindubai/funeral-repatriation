Brunei is one of the more straightforward Southeast Asian repatriation cases, with a resident British High Commission and a direct flight home. The one question that shapes everything else is religious classification.

## Why classification is the first question

Islam is Brunei's state religion, and the Malay Islamic Monarchy framework runs through much of public life, including how deaths are processed. Non-Muslim deaths go through the civil registry only, with a straightforward path to a death certificate in Malay and English. Muslim deaths also require clearance from the Department of Religious Affairs (Jabatan Hal Ehwal Ugama Islam) before any export permit can be issued. Neither path is dramatically slower than the other, but they're genuinely different processes, and knowing which applies from the outset avoids wasted steps.

## When police involvement changes the timeline

Accidents or sudden deaths without a prior medical history bring in the Royal Brunei Police Force. The Attorney General's Chambers can require an inquest before the death certificate is finalised. This typically adds 7 to 14 days on top of the standard process, and it applies regardless of religious classification, since it sits under criminal procedure rather than religious clearance.

## The route home is unusually direct

Royal Brunei Airlines flies directly between Brunei International Airport and London Heathrow. That's genuinely rare for this region, most Southeast Asian corridors need at least one connection, and it simplifies cargo booking considerably. Singapore Changi offers a fallback route via Singapore Airlines if scheduling doesn't line up.

## Military families: a separate track

British Garrison Brunei houses the 1st Battalion, Royal Gurkha Rifles among other units, and it's one of the UK's longest-standing overseas deployments. A death connected to the Garrison, whether personnel or a dependant, is handled through Ministry of Defence channels with a casualty notification officer assigned directly. This is a distinct process from the civilian repatriation route.
