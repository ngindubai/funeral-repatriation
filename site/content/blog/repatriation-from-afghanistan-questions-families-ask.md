Afghanistan is the most difficult repatriation case a UK family can face, and it needs saying plainly rather than softened. The FCDO advises against all travel to Afghanistan, the UK Embassy in Kabul has been closed since August 2021, and the country the UK deals with on paper is not one it formally recognises as a government.

## No resident embassy to call

Call the FCDO first, on +44 (0)20 7008 5000, available 24 hours. This is the essential first step, not an optional one. The British Embassy in Islamabad, Pakistan offers what consular assistance is possible for Afghanistan-connected cases, but there is no equivalent post inside the country itself.

## Documentation through an unrecognised government

Civil death certificates and export permits in Afghanistan are issued by Taliban-controlled authorities. The UK does not formally recognise the Taliban as Afghanistan's legitimate government, which leaves documentation from these authorities in a genuine grey area for UK consular purposes. In practice, firms with established contacts predating the current administration are the ones able to move a case through the relevant civil authority. This isn't paperwork a family, or even a general international funeral firm without Afghanistan-specific experience, can push through alone.

## Why the timeline is so wide

Twenty-eight to a hundred and twenty days, where repatriation is achievable at all. That range exists because security conditions, not administrative steps, set the pace. In parts of Kandahar, Helmand, Kunar, Nuristan, and the border regions, ground access to a body may simply not be possible or safe. The FCDO will give an honest assessment of what's currently achievable in a specific location, and that assessment should come before any assumption about timing.

## Burial custom and the speed of a decision

Islamic burial within 24 hours of death is the universal norm across Afghanistan. If a family wants repatriation rather than local burial, that intention needs to be made clear immediately to whoever is present, because the default local expectation runs the other way. Cremation is not available in the country, so repatriation of the body itself, not ashes, is the only route where it is chosen.
