Comoros doesn't come up often in repatriation conversations, but for the families it does affect, the first question isn't about paperwork. It's geography.

## Three islands, one working airport

Grande Comore, Anjouan, and Moheli each run their own local administration under the federal government of the Union of the Comoros, but only Grande Comore, where Moroni sits, has cargo-capable international departures. A death on either of the other two islands means transferring the body to Grande Comore first, usually by small charter plane or boat, adding a day or two before anything else can begin.

## Consular help comes from another country

There's no resident British Embassy here. The British High Commission in Port Louis, Mauritius covers Comoros as a non-resident responsibility, on +230 202 9400. It's a well-established arrangement given the country's size, but it does mean an extra link in the chain compared with a country that has its own Embassy on the ground.

## French law, with an Islamic layer

Civil registration in Comoros follows French civil law, supplemented by Islamic personal status law. The attending physician certifies cause of death, the civil registrar issues the acte de deces in French and Arabic, and for anything non-natural, the Gendarmerie Nationale has to attend and clear the case before the Ministry of Health can issue an export permit.

## Why the first day matters most

Comoros is almost entirely Sunni Muslim, and the pressure for prompt local burial is genuine and immediate. Families who want their loved one brought home instead need to say so clearly, and fast. Waiting a few days to decide can close options that were open on day one.

## Two connections minimum to reach the UK

From Prince Said Ibrahim International Airport in Moroni, there's no route to London without at least two connections: Air Austral via Reunion, Ethiopian Airlines via Addis Ababa, or Kenya Airways via Nairobi are the realistic options, each needing its own cargo coordination.
