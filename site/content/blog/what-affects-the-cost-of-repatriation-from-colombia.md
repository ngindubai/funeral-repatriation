Colombia has become a genuine draw for British travellers over the last decade, from Cartagena's old town to the Lost City trek. When something goes wrong, though, the route home is rarely simple.

## Where the death happened decides the first step

El Dorado Airport in Bogota handles almost all of Colombia's international cargo of this kind. A death in Cartagena, the coffee region, or anywhere outside the capital means a domestic transfer to Bogota before international export can start. It's the first thing worth knowing, because it changes the shape of the whole timeline.

## Two authorities, not one

For a natural death with documentation ready, fourteen days is a realistic minimum. An unnatural death brings in the Instituto de Medicina Legal for the forensic post-mortem, and for anything with a criminal dimension, the Fiscalia General de la Nacion has to sign off on release as well. Results in Bogota and Medellin typically arrive in seven to fourteen days; in smaller cities, it can run longer.

## A permit most people haven't heard of

INVIMA, Colombia's medicines and food regulator, also issues the health export permit for human remains. It's an easy step to miss if a funeral director hasn't handled a Latin America case before, and it can only start once the other documents are in place. Budget three to seven days for it once everything else clears.

## No direct route home

There's no nonstop flight from Colombia to the UK. Avianca or Copa Airlines usually cover the first leg, via Miami or Panama City, before a final connection on to Heathrow; Madrid is the other common option. Each connection has its own cargo booking window, and that's before allowing for weather or capacity on the day.
