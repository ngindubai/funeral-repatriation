Cuba isn't a country where a family gets to make many choices about how a repatriation unfolds. Almost every step is fixed by state process, and understanding that from the outset is the most useful thing anyone can do at the start.

## An autopsy with no exceptions

Medicina Legal, Cuba's state forensic authority, examines every foreign national who dies in the country. Not just the unexplained or violent cases: every one. There's no route to declining it, and results typically take 7 to 14 days. Nothing else in the process can move until that examination is complete and signed off, which makes it the single biggest driver of how long a Cuba case takes overall.

## One funeral service, no alternative

Cuba runs its funeral services through a state monopoly, the Empresa de Servicios Funerarios. Private options exist only at the margins. Embalming has to be carried out by state-approved personnel, full stop. For a family used to choosing a funeral director in the UK, this can feel unfamiliar, even unsettling. It isn't something a UK specialist can work around; it's simply how Cuba's system operates.

## What the Embassy can and can't fix

The British Embassy in Havana is the right first point of contact, and it will register the death and provide the paperwork a family needs on the UK side. What it cannot do is waive the Medicina Legal requirement or accelerate the export documentation that comes next. In an emergency, the FCDO's 24-hour line, 020 7008 5000, is the number to call first.

## The export paperwork

Once Medicina Legal has finished, Cuba's Ministry of Foreign Affairs, MINREX, issues the formal export package: a laissez-passer, an embalming certificate, and a sanitary clearance. This runs after the forensic stage, not alongside it, and typically takes a further 10 to 20 days.

## Getting home

There are no direct flights from Cuba to the UK. Most cases route from Havana to Madrid with Iberia before continuing on to a UK airport, though some go via Cancun or Toronto instead. Each transit country adds its own paperwork, which a specialist needs to coordinate at every stage, not just at the Cuban end.
