A heart attack is one of the most common reasons a British national dies abroad, and also one of the most variable in how long repatriation takes. The difference usually comes down to one question: was it witnessed.

## Witnessed and medically attended

If your loved one collapsed in front of others, paramedics or hospital staff attended, and there's a known history of heart disease, most countries treat this as a straightforward natural death. A doctor certifies the cause directly from the clinical picture. No post-mortem is required in this scenario in the majority of jurisdictions.

This is the fastest path through the process. Documentation can begin almost immediately, and repatriation to the UK typically completes in 5 to 14 days.

## Unwitnessed or unexplained

A different picture applies when someone is found alone, in a hotel room or at a property, with no witnesses and possibly no known cardiac history. Local authorities in most countries won't assume cardiac cause in this situation. They'll usually want a doctor, sometimes a police doctor or forensic examiner, to confirm there's nothing else at play before a certificate can be issued.

This can mean a post-mortem, though not always a full forensic one. Many countries use a limited external examination to rule out other causes before releasing the body, reserving a full post-mortem for cases with genuine ambiguity. Either way, this step adds time: typically several days to two weeks, depending on how the local system is set up and how busy the relevant department is.

It's a reasonable question to ask directly: why does a sudden death in an apparently healthy person get more scrutiny than one with a known history? The answer is straightforward. Authorities can't certify a cause they haven't confirmed, and a witnessed collapse with known heart disease gives them that confirmation immediately. An unwitnessed death doesn't.

## What the UK coroner does with it

Once the body arrives in England or Wales, the Coroners and Justice Act 2009 requires notification to the coroner for the area where the funeral is to take place. For a well-documented cardiac death, this is usually a document review: the coroner checks the foreign certificate and any post-mortem findings, confirms they're consistent with natural death, and authorises the funeral to proceed. This typically takes 1 to 5 working days and rarely triggers a UK-based post-mortem on top of any already carried out abroad.

Scotland and Northern Ireland handle this through the Procurator Fiscal and the Northern Ireland Coroner's Service respectively, with broadly similar outcomes for straightforward cardiac cases.

## Travel insurance and the paperwork

Most policies covering repatriation will ask for the medical or death certificate, and, where one was carried out, the post-mortem report, before releasing funds. This is standard practice, not a sign the claim is in question. Keep copies of everything the local doctor or authority issues; the funeral director handling the case abroad will usually assemble these on the family's behalf.
