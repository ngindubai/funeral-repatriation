The Okavango Delta draws British visitors precisely because it's remote: no roads, no towns, just water channels and bush camps reached by small aircraft. That same remoteness is the first thing a family needs to understand if a death happens there.

## Getting out of the Delta comes first

Bush camps, private concessions, and the Moremi Game Reserve have no road access whatsoever. A death anywhere in the Delta requires charter aircraft or helicopter evacuation to Maun Airport before documentation can start. Depending on the camp's location and aircraft availability, that evacuation alone takes 1 to 4 hours. Add ground or further air transfer to Gaborone for anything beyond a straightforward case, and the pre-documentation delay for a remote Delta death can run 24 to 96 hours before the formal process even opens.

## Where the forensic work happens

Princess Marina Hospital in Gaborone runs the Department of Forensic Pathology and handles the more complex post-mortems. Maun Hospital, serving the Okavango gateway, and Kasane Hospital, serving Chobe National Park, both have basic capability, but anything beyond routine cases still transfers south to Gaborone.

## No coastline, no shortcuts

Botswana is entirely landlocked. Every international cargo route runs through OR Tambo International Airport in Johannesburg, with Nairobi via Kenya Airways as a secondary option. Sir Seretse Khama International Airport in Gaborone is where the domestic and international legs connect; there's no way to avoid at least one African hub before the flight to Heathrow.

## What the British High Commission brings to a case

The High Commission in Gaborone has real experience with safari-related deaths, reflecting how many British visitors come for the Delta and Chobe specifically. English is the official language of Botswana's administration, so there's no translation delay layered on top of the logistics, which is one less thing to manage during an already difficult evacuation and documentation process.
