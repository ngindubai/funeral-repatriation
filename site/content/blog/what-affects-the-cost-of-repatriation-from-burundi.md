Burundi is small, landlocked, and rarely visited by British nationals in large numbers, which is exactly why few UK repatriation firms have live contacts there. The single biggest factor in how a Burundi case runs is not the death itself. It is the fact that British consular support sits in a different country altogether.

## A non-resident High Commission

The British High Commission covering Burundi is based in Kampala, Uganda, not Bujumbura. Death registration for UK purposes is coordinated remotely from Kampala, reachable on +256 31 231 2000, and while that arrangement is entirely workable, it is not the same as having consular staff who can walk into a Bujumbura office in person. A specialist firm with existing Burundian contacts closes that gap far faster than trying to build one from scratch mid-case.

## The Parquet has the final say on non-natural deaths

Death certification runs through Burundi's civil registry, the registrar des actes de l'etat civil, which issues the foundational document every later step depends on. Where a death is not straightforwardly natural, the Public Prosecutor's Office, the Parquet, has to authorise release before anything further can happen. This is a legal requirement, not paperwork that can be expedited by paying more.

## Kirundi and French, throughout

Every piece of Burundian documentation, from the civil registry certificate to the export permit, arrives in Kirundi or French. Certified English translation is needed for all of it before UK authorities will accept the paperwork. Building translation time into the plan from day one avoids a late surprise.

## Three routes out, one usually fastest

Bujumbura's Melchior Ndadaye International Airport connects onward via Nairobi (Kenya Airways, Ethiopian Airlines), Brussels (Brussels Airlines' direct service), or Addis Ababa (Ethiopian Airlines' wider network). Cremation is not widely available in Burundi, so full body repatriation is the standard route home rather than an exception.
