Guyana looks straightforward on paper. English is the official language, the legal system follows English common law, and the British High Commission in Georgetown is resident. Then you look at the map, and two-thirds of the country is jungle.

## What makes Guyana manageable

A resident British High Commission in Georgetown, English-language documentation throughout, and English common law procedures put Guyana well ahead of most South American repatriation destinations for a UK family. The General Register Office issues the death certificate once the attending physician has certified cause of death. For non-natural deaths, a post-mortem at Georgetown Public Hospital, and sometimes a Magistrate's inquest, comes first.

## Georgetown and the coastal strip

Around 90 percent of Guyana's population lives on the coastal strip, where medical, police, and funeral services function predictably. Road accidents, medical emergencies, and occasional violent incidents are the typical causes here, and Georgetown Public Hospital has established pathology services to handle them. Funeral directors in the city have real international repatriation experience, helped by the large Guyanese-UK diaspora.

## The interior is a different country

Gold and diamond mining in the Mazaruni, Essequibo, and Potaro regions brings industrial accidents, drownings, and falls. Kaieteur Falls, the Iwokrama Rainforest, and the Rupununi savanna draw eco-tourists, and cardiac events and accidents are the main causes there. Offshore, floating oil and gas platforms in Atlantic waters need maritime recovery procedures entirely of their own. Reaching any of these locations means charter light aircraft from Ogle Airport or a river boat journey on the Essequibo, Demerara, or Berbice. Jungle heat makes speed genuinely urgent for body preservation, so recovery has to start the moment a specialist firm with confirmed interior contacts is engaged.

## The route home

Cheddi Jagan International Airport, at Timehri south of Georgetown, has no direct UK service. Cargo typically follows one of four passenger routes: via Miami with American Airlines or Caribbean Airlines, via New York JFK, via Trinidad connecting to a Caribbean Airlines London Gatwick service, or via Barbados. Miami and Trinidad see the most UK-bound cargo traffic in practice.
