Liechtenstein doesn't have an airport. Not a small one, not a regional strip, none at all. It's one of only two doubly landlocked countries on earth, the other being Uzbekistan, entirely surrounded by Switzerland and Austria. That single geographic fact makes it, paradoxically, one of the fastest and simplest repatriation cases this service handles.

## Getting a body out without an airport

Every repatriation from Liechtenstein starts with a road transfer. Zürich Kloten sits around ninety minutes away and carries multiple daily flights to UK airports, making it the standard routing. Basel-Mulhouse, roughly two hours by road, is the usual alternative. A specialist firm arranges this transfer as part of the overall case. Because the roads are excellent and the distance short, it adds very little time compared with countries where the nearest international airport is hours or days away.

## A German-language, German-style legal system

Liechtenstein runs on a German-influenced civil law system. The Amt für Bevölkerung und Zivilregister, the Population and Civil Registry Office, issues the death certificate, in German. Where a death isn't from natural causes, the Landgericht, the Regional Court, has to authorise release before anything else can proceed. Every document needs certified translation into English before the UK Coroner can act on it, the one real administrative step in an otherwise clean process.

## What the timeline looks like

Most cases run three to ten days from death to repatriation, among the shortest timelines in Europe. Stable governance, a functioning court system, and that short road transfer to Zürich all play a part. Cremation is also available locally, and returning ashes rather than the body is a practical option some families choose instead.
