A death in China puts a family through more official steps than almost anywhere else this site covers. None of them are optional, and most run one after another rather than side by side.

## No shared framework to shorten the process

China has never signed the Strasbourg Agreement, the international convention several European corridors rely on for a common set of rules. Without it, everything rests on Chinese domestic law, administered through the Ministry of Civil Affairs. That single fact is why a China case takes structurally longer than a comparable one in, say, Germany or Poland.

## The Public Security Bureau decides the pace

For any death that isn't straightforwardly natural, the Public Security Bureau opens its own investigation before a post-mortem is even ordered. Nothing else can proceed until it closes the case and releases the body. In complicated instances that step alone runs two to four weeks. There's no way to speed a PSB investigation from the outside; a family's specialist can only make sure the release, once it comes, is acted on immediately.

## An approved mortuary, not just any mortuary

Embalming has to happen at a facility approved by the local Civil Affairs Bureau. Coverage varies by province, and not every region has one that meets the standard required for international transfer. This is why the British Embassy Beijing, or the relevant Consulate-General in Shanghai, Guangzhou, Chengdu, or Wuhan, needs to confirm the right facility before a family agrees to anything locally.

## Two more permits before departure

Once the body is cleared and embalmed, Chinese Customs issues the Jian Yi Zheng Shu, the health clearance certificate confirming the remains are free from contagious disease. Separately, the exit permit itself comes from the local Civil Affairs Bureau, and every Chinese-language document behind it needs certified translation, sometimes authentication through the Ministry of Foreign Affairs too.

## The flight is the easy part

Once all of that clears, getting home is simple. British Airways and Virgin Atlantic fly direct, daily, from Beijing Capital and Shanghai Pudong straight to Heathrow. Air China also serves the route. It's one of the few genuinely straightforward stages in the whole process.
