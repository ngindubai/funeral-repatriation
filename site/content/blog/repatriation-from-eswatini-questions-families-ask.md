Eswatini, renamed from Swaziland in 2018, is a country many families haven't heard much about before a death brings it into their lives. It's small, landlocked, tucked between South Africa and Mozambique, and for repatriation purposes it's genuinely one of the more manageable Southern African cases. English is an official language throughout. That single fact removes a whole layer of difficulty that families face elsewhere in the region.

## Who typically dies here

Most British deaths in Eswatini involve tourists at Hlane Royal National Park or Mkhaya Game Reserve, business travellers, NGO and development sector staff, and members of the small but established UK-Swazi diaspora community. It's not a high-volume corridor, but the British High Commission in Mbabane, a resident mission, is familiar with the process.

## Registering the death

The Registrar General's Office handles registration once the attending physician has certified cause of death, and the certificate itself is issued in English. Where a death isn't from natural causes, the Royal Eswatini Police Service has to attend and clear the case first, which typically adds 5 to 10 working days for a routine accident. Anything drawing a fuller criminal investigation takes longer, and there's no way to give a family a firm date until that investigation is under way.

## The Ministry of Health permit

Once the death certificate exists, the Ministry of Health issues the export permit for human remains. This part of the process is genuinely straightforward in Eswatini, usually completed within 2 to 3 working days.

## A monarchy, and what that means practically

Eswatini is the last absolute monarchy anywhere in sub-Saharan Africa. King Mswati III holds executive, legislative, and judicial power, which means there's no independent courts process a family could turn to if a case stalled somewhere along the way. It sounds daunting, but in practice it simply means the relationship a specialist firm has with local funeral directors and government contacts matters more here than in a country with a more conventional legal separation of powers.

## Getting home

Every case travels via Johannesburg O.R. Tambo International Airport; there's no direct Eswatini-UK service. From King Mswati III Airport or Matsapha Airport, cargo connects to JNB and then on to London Heathrow with British Airways, South African Airways, or Virgin Atlantic, all well established for this kind of transit.
