The Democratic Republic of Congo asks a different question from almost every other country on this site before anything else: where, precisely, did the death occur. The answer changes not just the timeline, but whether repatriation is achievable at all.

## Kinshasa: difficult, but genuinely possible

The British Embassy is resident in Kinshasa and fully operational, registering deaths and issuing the notifications a UK case needs. From there, the process runs through Police Nationale Congolaise attendance for non-natural deaths, forensic certification from the Institut National Medico-Legal, Parquet clearance confirming no criminal prosecution is pending, a civil registry death certificate, and a Ministry of Health export permit, in that order.

## Where the delay actually sits

The Institut National Medico-Legal is the single biggest bottleneck. Its capacity is limited relative to the demands of a city of fifteen million people, and forensic certification for non-natural deaths typically takes seven to twenty-one days on its own. Add the remaining steps and twenty-one to forty-two days is the realistic range for a standard Kinshasa case.

## Eastern DRC is a different situation entirely

North Kivu, South Kivu, and Ituri province have faced active armed conflict, involving the M23 movement, the Armed Forces of the DRC, and numerous other armed groups, for years. The FCDO advises against all travel to these areas, and to zones within 50 kilometres of the DRC's borders with Rwanda and Uganda in North Kivu specifically. Scheduled air service to Goma suspends during active fighting, and recovering a body from a combat area needs one of the very small number of specialist crisis recovery firms operating in the region. In some cases, repatriation simply isn't achievable through any civilian process, and families deserve to hear that plainly rather than being given false reassurance.

## Getting home from Kinshasa

Once documentation clears, Brussels Airlines runs a direct service from Ndjili International Airport to Brussels and is the most reliable route for DRC-UK cargo. Ethiopian Airlines via Addis Ababa and Kenya Airways via Nairobi are the alternatives when the direct route isn't practical.
