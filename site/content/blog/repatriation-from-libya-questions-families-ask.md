Libya sits in a category of its own among the corridors this service handles. There's no single functioning national government with consistent authority nationwide. There's no resident British Embassy. Armed groups hold significant territory. And still, British nationals die in Libya, oil workers, journalists, NGO staff, contractors among them, and their families need to bring them home. This page sets out what that process genuinely looks like, without softening it.

## Why the political map matters more than the paperwork

Since 2014, Libya has had competing governments and multiple armed factions holding different parts of the country. The Government of National Unity nominally runs Tripoli and the west. The Libyan National Army controls much of the east, Benghazi included. Fezzan, in the south, has barely any central government presence, with armed groups, including remnants of Islamic State, active in places. What follows from this: there's no single Ministry of Health to issue an export permit, and the authority who can issue a death certificate depends entirely on which territory the death occurred in.

## What British consular support can and can't do

Tripoli's British Embassy closed in 2014. Support now comes remotely from British Embassy Tunis. That means no UK official can attend the scene of a death in-country, registration happens by phone and email, and any family member considering travelling into Libya to help would be doing so without UK consular protection once there, something we would never recommend.

## The documentation problem, stated plainly

In a functioning state, a municipal registry issues the death certificate, a government facility carries out any forensic examination, and a health ministry signs the export clearance. In Libya, the authority controlling the relevant municipality issues the certificate instead. Forensic capacity is thin and varies by location. Export clearance means negotiating with whoever controls the ground, which can include unofficial fees. Every document is in Arabic and needs certified translation. This isn't paperwork in the ordinary sense. It's a negotiation with armed actors, and it needs people who understand those actors and speak the relevant dialects.

Call the FCDO Emergency Travel Line straight away: 020 7008 5000, available at any hour. Don't attempt to enter Libya yourself. Contact British Embassy Tunis to start formal registration, and notify the employer or insurer, since most Libya cases are insurer-led from the outset.
