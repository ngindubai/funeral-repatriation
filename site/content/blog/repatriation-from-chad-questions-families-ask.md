N'Djamena runs on a functioning civil process. Most of the rest of the country doesn't, and that gap is the first thing a family needs to understand about repatriation from Chad.

## A government still finding its footing

Chad has been under a Transitional Military Council since President Idriss Deby was killed in April 2021. His son, Mahamat Idriss Deby, has led it since. A new constitution was approved by referendum in 2024, and elections are planned, but the administrative machinery hasn't fully settled into a permanent shape yet. Practically, that means routine steps, such as issuing a death certificate through the etat civil, can take longer under a transitional authority than they would under an established government.

## Consular coverage from Yaounde

There is no resident British Embassy in Chad. Every consular matter, including registering a death for UK purposes, goes through the British Embassy in Yaounde, Cameroon, which holds non-resident accreditation. Call the FCDO Emergency Travel Line first, on 020 7008 5000, available 24 hours. The Yaounde Embassy can be reached directly on +237 222 220 545.

French and Arabic are Chad's official languages. Every civil document, including the acte de deces issued once the police and (where required) the Tribunal correctionnel have cleared the case, needs certified English translation before it's of any use in the UK.

## The line the FCDO draws

The FCDO advises against all travel to northern Chad (Tibesti and Borkou) and to the eastern and southern border regions, and against all but essential travel to the rest of the country, N'Djamena included. Eastern Chad is absorbing real spillover from the conflict in Sudan, including a refugee population that strains what infrastructure exists locally.

For a family, the practical consequence is blunt: N'Djamena is where a case gets done. Outside it, standard civilian recovery isn't realistic, and any specialist worth engaging will say so plainly rather than promise otherwise.

## Getting a body out

Hassan Djamous N'Djamena International Airport handles the departure. Three routes matter. Ethiopian Airlines via Addis Ababa is currently the steadiest choice. Air France flies N'Djamena to Paris CDG directly, connecting on to London. Royal Air Maroc via Casablanca is a third option worth having in reserve.
