If your loved one wanted to be cremated, and they died in Egypt, that can't happen there. It's the first thing worth knowing, and it shapes every decision that follows.

## No cremation, no exceptions

Egypt has no cremation facilities anywhere in the country. This isn't about cost or convenience. It follows directly from Egypt's religious and legal framework, and there's no workaround. Full body repatriation to the UK is the only path if cremation is what the family has in mind. For families whose loved one was Muslim, the same framework works the other way: burial as soon as possible after death is expected, and Egyptian authorities generally move quickly once the family's wishes are clear.

## Arabic paperwork, then a stamp from Cairo

Every official document, including the shahadat wafaa, the death certificate, is issued in Arabic only. A certified English translation has to follow, and the Ministry of Foreign Affairs must stamp the certificate before it carries any international legal weight. Neither step is optional.

## The niyaba, and why it isn't a red flag

Virtually every tourist death in Egypt counts as unnatural, sudden, or suspicious under local law, which means the niyaba, the public prosecutor, orders a post-mortem as a matter of routine. It typically adds five to ten days. Release depends on the niyaba's authorisation, not on anything a family or a funeral director can accelerate.

## Where the body died still matters

Hurghada and Sharm el-Sheikh see British deaths regularly, and local funeral directors there know the process. But there's no British consulate in either resort; everything routes through the Embassy in Cairo, 500 kilometres away, and response times reflect that distance. Embalming quality is high in Cairo and more variable in the resorts, which is why some cases transfer to Cairo for preparation before the flight home.
