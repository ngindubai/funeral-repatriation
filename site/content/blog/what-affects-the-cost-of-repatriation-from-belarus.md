A death in Belarus rarely has a simple flight home attached to it anymore. That single change, the loss of a direct route, touches nearly every case, and it isn't the only thing families need to plan around.

## The end of direct flights

Direct services between the UK and Belarus stopped in 2021, after the forced diversion of Ryanair flight FR4978. They haven't restarted. Every repatriation now routes through a connecting hub: Warsaw with LOT Polish Airlines, Vilnius, or Istanbul with Turkish Airlines. Each of those adds a transit leg, a second set of handling arrangements, and time that a direct service simply wouldn't need.

## When the Investigative Committee gets involved

Not every case is complicated. A natural death with clear documentation moves relatively quickly through the Civil Registry Department, the Belarusian equivalent of a registrar's office, once the attending physician has certified the cause. But anything sudden, violent, or unexplained brings in the Investigative Committee of Belarus, which must clear the case, confirming no prosecution is pending, before the death certificate can be issued. A straightforward accident typically adds 10 to 20 days here. A case with any complication, dual nationality, or a connection to political activity among them, becomes far harder to put a number on.

## A smaller Embassy, doing more with less

The British Embassy in Minsk continues to operate, but with significantly reduced staff following the deterioration in the diplomatic relationship since 2020. It still registers deaths and issues the notifications UK families need for probate purposes. It just has less capacity to move quickly than a fully staffed post would.

## Why families shouldn't travel out themselves

FCDO guidance advises against all but essential travel to Belarus, and British nationals there face a heightened risk of detention. We understand the instinct to go and handle things in person. In this particular case, it genuinely is safer, and usually faster, to coordinate everything remotely through the Embassy and a specialist firm with existing Eastern European contacts.
