Saint Vincent and the Grenadines is a chain rather than a single island, and that shapes almost everything about how a repatriation case unfolds. The main island of Saint Vincent sits alongside Mustique, Bequia, Canouan, and Union Island to the south, each with its own logistics to think through if a death happens there.

## The main island is genuinely straightforward

For deaths on Saint Vincent itself, this is one of the more manageable Caribbean cases on this site. English is the official language throughout, common law governs civil registration, and the Registrar General's Office issues the death certificate without any translation step. Where death wasn't from natural causes, the Coroner's Court has to certify the case before release, standard practice across the Eastern Caribbean.

## The Grenadines change the logistics, not the law

Mustique, Bequia, Canouan, and Union Island are all part of the same jurisdiction, so the legal process doesn't change. What does change is getting the body to where that process can actually happen. A death on any of these islands needs an internal transfer, by inter-island ferry or light aircraft, to Saint Vincent's Argyle International Airport before international repatriation can be arranged. It's a coordination step, and a specialist firm treats it as part of one continuous process rather than a separate booking that risks falling through the gaps.

## Getting home

Most UK-bound routing goes via Barbados, using Grantley Adams International, with onward connections from there. Caribbean Airlines and LIAT cover the inter-island network that gets people, and remains, to that connection point.

There are no significant FCDO travel advisories for the country, and cremation is available on the main island for families who prefer it to full repatriation.
