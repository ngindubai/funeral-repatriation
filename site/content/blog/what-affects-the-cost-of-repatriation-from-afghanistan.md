There's no getting around it: Afghanistan is the most complex, and realistically the most costly, repatriation origin a UK family can face. Understanding why helps you make sense of any quote you receive, and helps you ask the right questions of the firm giving it to you.

## The embassy closure changes everything

The UK Embassy in Kabul has been closed since August 2021, and the UK does not formally recognise the Taliban as Afghanistan's legitimate government. That single fact reshapes the entire process. Documentation has to be obtained through Taliban-controlled civil authorities, and consular support runs instead through the British Embassy in Islamabad, Pakistan, at a distance. Every extra layer of coordination has a cost attached to it, whether that's staff time, courier fees, or specialist local knowledge that took years to build.

## Few firms can even take the case

Very few specialist repatriation firms maintain active operations in Afghanistan. A firm with genuine, current in-country contacts, rather than a historic presence from before 2021, is doing something that most competitors in this space simply cannot offer. That capability isn't free to maintain, and it shows in what a case from Afghanistan costs compared with, say, a case from France.

## Islamic burial and speed

Afghanistan is almost entirely Muslim, and burial within 24 hours is universally observed. When repatriation is the family's choice instead, that intention has to be communicated immediately, and moving fast under pressure is rarely the cheapest way to do anything. Expedited coordination costs more than a case with time to plan properly, and Afghanistan frequently allows no time at all.

## Where the security situation adds cost

In areas of active conflict, including parts of Kandahar, Helmand, Kunar, and Nuristan, access to the deceased may simply not be possible for a ground operator, safely or otherwise. Where it is possible, it often requires security coordination that a straightforward case elsewhere would never need. Translation of Dari and Pashto documents into English adds a further, unavoidable layer.

Cremation isn't available in Afghanistan, so full body repatriation via Dubai, the primary connection hub for onward UK cargo, is the only route, and commercial air access itself is limited and subject to frequent change.
