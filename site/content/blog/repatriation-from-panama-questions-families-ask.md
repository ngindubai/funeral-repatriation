Panama draws a wider mix of British visitors than most of its neighbours: backpackers passing through on the route between South America and Costa Rica, retirees settled under the Pensionado visa scheme, cruise passengers stopping to see the canal, and hikers heading to the Chiriqui Highlands around Boquete.

## Why Panama is comparatively straightforward

Two things set Panama apart from most of Central America. First, the British Embassy is resident in Panama City itself, so families aren't dealing with a non-resident post covering several countries at once. Second, Tocumen International Airport (PTY) is one of the region's major cargo hubs, with strong connections to Miami, Madrid, and Bogota. That combination shortens both the consular and the logistics side of the process.

Unnatural deaths, including accidents and drownings, go to the Instituto de Medicina Legal y Ciencias Forenses. This is the country's main forensic pathology body, and its involvement adds roughly four to six weeks rather than the two to three weeks a straightforward natural death takes through the Registro Civil.

## Where geography complicates things

Bocas del Toro, the Caribbean-coast archipelago popular with backpackers for surfing and snorkelling, has no bridge to the mainland. A death there means a water taxi to Almirante followed by a road journey to David or Changuinola before documentation can start. The Chiriqui Highlands, home to the eco-tourism and coffee-farm circuit around Boquete, need a shorter road transfer, roughly 40 minutes to David, where the regional hospital handles the case.

The Darien Gap is a different problem entirely. It has no road connections at all, so any death there requires helicopter evacuation and involves Panamanian border security alongside the usual repatriation steps. These cases are rare, but they are genuinely complex when they occur.

All Spanish-language documentation needs certified English translation before UK authorities will accept it, whatever region the death occurred in.
