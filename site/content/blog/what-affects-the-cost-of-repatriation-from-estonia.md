Estonia doesn't get much attention on this site, mostly because it doesn't need to. It's one of the more manageable countries a family can be dealing with after a sudden loss.

## An unusually digital civil service

Death registration runs through the Rahvastikuregister, the Population Register, administered by the Ministry of the Interior. Estonia built its reputation on e-governance for a reason: officials issue death certificates efficiently, without the paper backlogs that slow things down elsewhere in Europe. That efficiency is the biggest single reason a straightforward Estonian case tends to move fast.

## Translation is still a real step

The certificate itself is issued in Estonian, and it needs certified translation into English before the UK Coroner will accept it. This is standard for any non-English-speaking EU country and shouldn't come as a surprise, but it's still worth building into the plan from day one rather than treating it as a last-minute task.

## When the police get involved

Non-natural deaths bring in the Police and Border Guard Board, the Politsei- ja Piirivalveamet, before release can happen. Because Estonia's administration runs efficiently across the board, this step tends not to create the kind of drag it might in a country with a slower or more paper-based system.

## A short flight, run often

Tallinn Lennart Meri Airport has direct Ryanair and easyJet flights to London Gatwick and Stansted, about three hours in the air. That frequency means capacity is rarely the reason a case waits.
