The Gambia is the smallest country on mainland Africa, and for winter sun visitors from the UK, one of the busiest. That volume means the system for handling a British death here is genuinely practised, not improvised.

## Why this corridor is more manageable

English is the official language. The Gambia is a Commonwealth member. The British High Commission in Banjul is resident, not covering the country from a neighbouring capital the way so many West African corridors work. Every document, from the death certificate through to the export permit, is produced in English. None of that removes the grief. It does remove a layer of friction most families in this position have to deal with elsewhere.

## Who to call first

Start with the British High Commission in Banjul and the FCDO Emergency Travel Line, 020 7008 5000, available around the clock. A specialist UK repatriation company then takes over the practical logistics from there.

## What causes most cases

Cardiac events in older tourists, road accidents on the South Bank Road, drownings, and sudden illness at coastal hotels account for most British deaths here. Natural deaths register at the local civil registry. Anything unnatural goes to the coroner, with post-mortem work at Edward Francis Small Teaching Hospital in Banjul, the country's forensic referral centre. Its capacity is limited, so a coroner case can still run three to five weeks.

## Coastal against upcountry

Most visitors stay on the coastal strip between Kololi and Bakau, and deaths here follow the fastest path: 10 to 14 days once the paperwork is straightforward. The Central River, Upper River, and North Bank regions are a different picture. Road transfer to Banjul takes time on its own, and North Bank cases add a river crossing on top. Allow four to six weeks for anything upcountry.

## Getting home

Banjul International Airport, also known as Yundum, receives direct UK charter flights from Manchester, Birmingham, Gatwick, and Bristol during the winter season. Outside peak months, some cases route via Dakar on the Senegalese network instead.
