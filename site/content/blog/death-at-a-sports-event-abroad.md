Marathons, club rugby tours, sailing regattas, five-a-side tournaments played on a works trip: British participation in organised sport abroad is large, and deaths during these events are not as rare as families assume until it happens to someone they know. The complication that catches people out first is rarely the process. It's the insurance.

## The gap most families don't check for

Standard travel insurance was built around holidays, not competition. MoneyHelper's guidance is clear that adventure sports, winter sports, and other activities insurers treat as high risk are commonly excluded from ordinary policies. Competing at national level, or on a professional or semi-professional basis, can exclude cover entirely under some policies, regardless of the sport itself.

This matters because the exclusion isn't always obvious from the policy summary. It's worth reading the specific activity wording, not just the headline cover amount, and doing this before travel rather than after a death has occurred.

## Who actually certifies the death

An event's own medical team, however well equipped, doesn't issue a death certificate. Their job is emergency response: resuscitation attempts, stabilisation, getting the person to hospital. Once local emergency services are involved, certification follows the same path as any other death in that country: a hospital doctor, a coroner-equivalent authority, or the police, depending on the circumstances and where the country's system draws that line.

## Organiser cover is not a given

Some events carry organiser-arranged insurance. Many don't, or the cover that exists is limited to medical treatment on the day rather than anything that follows, including repatriation. GOV.UK's own guidance on foreign travel insurance puts the responsibility back on the traveller: check, before you go, that your own policy names the activity you're taking part in. Don't assume an event's entry fee includes cover broad enough for what happens after a death.

A club or tour operator will often help practically after the fact, liaising with local police or hospital staff, passing on information, supporting the family through what can be an unfamiliar system. That's genuinely useful. It isn't the same as funding, and clubs aren't obliged to cover repatriation costs unless their own terms say so.

## What to do in the first hours

Find the travel insurance policy. Separately, ask the event organiser in writing whether any organiser cover applies and exactly what it includes. Call the FCDO on +44 (0)20 7008 5000 for consular support whatever the insurance position turns out to be.
