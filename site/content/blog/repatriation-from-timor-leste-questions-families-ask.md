Timor-Leste became independent in 2002, after more than two decades of Indonesian occupation that followed the end of Portuguese colonial rule. It's one of the world's youngest states, and its civil registration system, while it does function, still reflects that recent history. Processes can move less predictably here than in longer-established countries.

For a UK family, one fact matters above the rest: there is no British Embassy in Timor-Leste. Consular help comes from the Australian Embassy in Dili, by arrangement between the UK and Australian governments, and their staff are experienced in the role.

## Getting the right help fast

Call the FCDO Emergency Travel Line first, 020 7008 5000, available 24 hours. Then the Australian Embassy in Dili, +670 332 2111. Be explicit that you're calling about a British national. The Australian Embassy handles death registration for UK purposes and liaises with Timorese authorities on your behalf, the same as it would for one of its own nationals.

## How death registration works

The Civil Registration Service issues the death certificate, in Portuguese and Tetum, once the attending physician has certified cause of death. For anything non-natural, the National Police of Timor-Leste, the PNTL, has to attend before the body can be moved. From there, a Ministry of Health export permit and certified English translations complete the document set.

## When the death is outside Dili

Timor-Leste is small geographically, but mortuary and medical facilities outside Dili are genuinely limited. A death in Baucau, Liquica, Manufahi, or anywhere else in the districts means transporting the body to Dili first, which typically takes 2 to 8 hours depending on road conditions. The Oecusse enclave is the hardest case on the map: it sits inside Indonesian West Timor, cut off from the rest of the country by land. Getting a body out means either a road route through West Timor or a charter flight from the local Oecusse airstrip.

## The route home

From Dili's Presidente Nicolau Lobato International Airport, the most commonly used cargo route runs via Bali on TransNusa or Garuda, then Singapore, then London. Darwin, via Airnorth, then Sydney, is the alternative if the Bali route isn't available. Either way, expect at least two connections before the case reaches the UK.

Given how young Timor-Leste's institutions still are, build some flexibility into your expectations. The Australian Embassy's direct working relationships with Timorese authorities are the biggest asset in resolving anything unusual quickly, and a firm with South-East Asian or Pacific experience will know how to use that.
