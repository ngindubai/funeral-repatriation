Dominica calls itself the Nature Isle, and it earns the name. Rainforest, hot springs, a volcanic spine running the length of the island: it's a place people come to for the landscape itself, not resort beaches. That same landscape is exactly what makes a repatriation from here different from most of its Caribbean neighbours.

Do not confuse it with the Dominican Republic. They are separate countries, separate systems, separate everything except a similar name.

## The legal side is the easy part

Dominica is a Commonwealth nation with English common law, and every document, from the medical certificate to the death certificate, is issued in English. The Registrar General's Office handles registration. Where a death isn't from natural causes, the Coroner's Court has to clear the case before anything moves forward. Nothing here needs translation, which is one less thing for a grieving family to manage.

## Where the difficulty actually lies

It's the terrain. Dominica's interior is some of the most rugged ground in the Caribbean: steep volcanic slopes, dense forest, roads that switch back on themselves for miles. A death in Roseau or one of the main coastal settlements moves through the process without much friction. A death further inland is a different matter entirely. Reaching Douglas-Charles Airport on the north-east coast can take hours on roads that aren't built for speed, and a firm with real local ground contacts makes a measurable difference here.

## No cremation, ever

There's no crematorium anywhere on the island. Whatever a family's wishes might normally be, full body repatriation is the only route home from Dominica. It's worth knowing this early, before any conversation about arrangements begins.

## Getting home

Douglas-Charles Airport's short runway limits it to smaller regional aircraft, so nothing leaves Dominica direct. Every route connects onward through Antigua or Barbados, where a UK-bound flight picks up the case. The High Commission in Bridgetown, non-resident but experienced with these cases, coordinates the consular side from there.

Documents you'll need along the way: a death certificate from the Registrar General, a Coroner's certificate if applicable, an embalming certificate, a freedom-from-infection certificate, and the export permit itself.
