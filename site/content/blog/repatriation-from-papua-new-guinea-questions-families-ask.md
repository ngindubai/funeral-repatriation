Papua New Guinea is, honestly, one of the harder cases a UK family can face. Not because the paperwork is unusual, but because the country itself is difficult to move through, and because some deaths become entangled in processes that have nothing to do with formal law at all.

## Notify the High Commission immediately

PNG does have a resident British High Commission in Port Moresby, unlike several of its Pacific neighbours, and it should be the first point of contact alongside the FCDO Emergency Travel Line. Waiting to see how things unfold before making that call is a mistake families sometimes make, and it costs time they don't get back.

## The geography works against speed

Road connectivity between provinces is minimal. Getting from Port Moresby to Mount Hagen, the main Highlands hub, requires a flight. Reaching more remote Highlands provinces, Enga, Hela, Southern Highlands, Gulf, means small charter aircraft to unprepared airstrips, and PNG's near-constant Highlands rainfall can ground those flights for days. That evacuation phase alone typically adds three to seven days before documentation can even begin.

## Urban risk and forensic capacity

Port Moresby and Lae both have high rates of violent crime, and the FCDO advises against travel to informal settlements in either city. Where a death results from a criminal incident, the Royal Papua New Guinea Constabulary's forensic resources are limited, and the British High Commission's involvement becomes essential rather than optional.

## The part families don't expect

With over 800 distinct language groups, PNG has customary law traditions that operate independently of the formal justice system. A death that a community perceives as involving sorcery, or one connected to tribal conflict, can trigger processes that delay or bypass ordinary investigation entirely. This is documented and recurring, not an edge case invented for this page, and it's the single biggest reason PNG needs specialist handling from the outset.
