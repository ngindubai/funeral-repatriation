It feels like a small thing to think about, and most families put it off. That's fine. There's no urgency.

## Why HM Passport Office says wait

A British passport doesn't need to be cancelled the moment someone dies. HM Passport Office's guidance on deceased persons' passports is direct about this: don't cancel it straight away, particularly if the death happened abroad. The document may still be required during repatriation, sometimes by the funeral director handling the paperwork, sometimes by local border officials who want to see it before the body can be exported.

Cancel it after the body has been repatriated, or after burial or cremation has taken place abroad if that's the family's choice. At that point there's no operational reason to keep it active.

## The D1 form

This is HM Passport Office's death notification form. It travels with the passport, or with the death certificate if the passport has been lost or is being held by local authorities abroad, to the Customer Service Management Team at HM Passport Office's Southport office.

The form asks a straightforward question: does the family want the passport returned once it's cancelled, or destroyed. There's no wrong answer. Some families keep it as a document; others would rather it wasn't sent back. Say which one you want when you send it, because the office won't ask twice.

## Tell Us Once and why it might not fit

If a death is registered in the UK, the Tell Us Once service reports it to several government departments in a single pass, HM Passport Office among them. For a death abroad, though, the death is registered first with the local authority in the country where it happened, not with a UK registrar. That's the legal requirement everywhere; it comes before anything else.

Because of that sequencing, Tell Us Once doesn't always slot in the same way for an overseas death. Where it doesn't apply, sending the D1 form directly to Southport is the route that works, and it works whether or not the death has been separately registered in the UK.

## What the embassy's role is here

HM Passport Office's own guidance says a death outside the UK should be dealt with at the nearest British Embassy, High Commission or Consulate first. That's about local coordination, not about them handling the passport paperwork for you. Staff can tell you whether the country where the death happened wants to see the passport as part of releasing the body, which varies. Some don't ask for it at all; others require it to confirm identity before issuing an exit permit.

## A practical order to do things in

Get through the local death registration and repatriation first. Send the D1 form to HM Passport Office once that's done, or once burial or cremation abroad has taken place if the body isn't coming home. There's genuinely no rush before that point, and trying to sort the passport out in the first frantic days usually just adds a task nobody has capacity for.
