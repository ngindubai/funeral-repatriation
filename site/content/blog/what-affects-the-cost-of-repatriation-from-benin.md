Benin sits in the moderate-complexity range for repatriation, and two things do most of the work in pushing a case toward the longer, costlier end of that range: the legal process, and where in the country the death occurred.

## A French legal model, two authorities involved

Death registration in Benin runs through the Tribunal de Premiere Instance, the local court, which handles the Acte de Deces itself. Before any export can be authorised, though, the Parquet, the public prosecutor's office, has to issue a separate clearance confirming no criminal investigation is pending. Neither authority substitutes for the other. Both have to complete before a repatriation firm can take the case further.

## Consular support, but from another country

There's no resident British diplomat in Cotonou. Families rely on the British High Commission in Accra, Ghana, which covers Benin on a non-resident basis. It's a workable arrangement, and one used across several West African countries, but it adds a coordination step that a resident Embassy wouldn't need.

## Geography changes everything in the north

Cotonou and the south are moderate risk, with functional infrastructure and an established documentation process. The Alibori and Atacora departments in the north are a different picture entirely. The FCDO advises against all travel within 5km of the Burkina Faso border, where Sahel-linked security activity has extended into northern Benin since 2022. A death there can require specialist logistics, and in some cases police or military coordination, before the deceased can even be reached.

## No local cremation, at any price

Cremation simply isn't practised in Benin. There are no facilities. A family who wants cremation has to repatriate the body to the UK first, then arrange it here, which is worth knowing early rather than discovering partway through a case.
