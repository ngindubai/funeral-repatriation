Burundi is a small, landlocked country bordering Rwanda, Tanzania, and the Democratic Republic of Congo, and its repatriation process is shaped as much by where the nearest British diplomatic post sits as by anything happening inside the country itself.

## Consular support comes from across a border

The British High Commission in Kampala, Uganda, handles every consular matter connected to Burundi. There's no resident post in Bujumbura. That non-resident arrangement means the usual back-and-forth of registering a death and coordinating with local authorities happens with an extra layer of distance built in. A specialist repatriation firm with established contacts inside Burundi can close that gap far more effectively than a family trying to manage it directly from the UK.

## The legal process itself

Burundi follows French civil law tradition. The civil registry, the registrar des actes de l'état civil, issues the death certificate that everything else builds on. Where the death wasn't from natural causes, the Parquet, Burundi's Public Prosecutor's Office, must authorise release before the body can be moved at all. Documentation comes in Kirundi and French, so certified English translation is required before it has any standing with UK authorities.

## Getting home from Bujumbura

Melchior Ndadaye International Airport is the country's only international gateway. Three routes serve UK-bound cargo: Kenya Airways or Ethiopian Airlines through Nairobi, Brussels Airlines direct to Brussels, or Ethiopian Airlines through its wider Addis Ababa network. None offer a direct UK service, so every case involves at least one connection.

## Full repatriation, not cremation

Cremation facilities aren't widely available in Burundi. Full body repatriation to the UK is the standard route for essentially every case. Knowing this from the start avoids building a plan around an option that isn't actually on the table.
