Prague draws a steady stream of British visitors for city breaks and skiing, and when a death does happen there, the Czech Republic's EU membership and Strasbourg Agreement status make it one of the more efficient corridors on this site.

## A shared framework, not just domestic rules

As an EU member and signatory to the Strasbourg Agreement, the Czech Republic's repatriation process runs on a structure that several countries recognise together, rather than one built entirely from local law. That shared framework, requiring embalming and a laissez-passer for international transfer, is a real part of why natural-cause cases with documentation ready typically complete in seven to ten days.

## Where the State Prosecutor comes in

Sudden or unnatural deaths go to the State Prosecutor, who orders a forensic post-mortem through the Institute of Forensic Medicine. Prague's facility is well resourced, and results usually arrive within five to ten days. Outside the capital, regional institutes have adequate capacity but can run somewhat slower, so location within the country still matters even in an otherwise efficient system.

## Translation, planned in from the start

Every Czech document, from the Umrtni list onward, is issued in Czech, and every one needs certified translation before it's of use for UK purposes. It's a small step on its own, but treating it as an afterthought rather than building it in early is one of the more common ways a straightforward case loses time.

## The laissez-passer and what comes before it

Once the death certificate, embalming certificate, freedom from contagious disease clearance, and laissez-passer from the Ministry of Interior are all in order, typically two to five days for the laissez-passer itself once the rest is ready, the body can be booked as air cargo.

## A genuine logistical advantage

Vaclav Havel Airport Prague runs frequent direct flights to Heathrow, Gatwick, and Stansted with British Airways, easyJet, and Ryanair. That frequency means cargo capacity is rarely the bottleneck once the documentation clears, which isn't something every European corridor on this site can say.
