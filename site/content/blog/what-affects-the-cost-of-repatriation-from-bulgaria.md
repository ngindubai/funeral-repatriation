Bulgaria's membership of the Council of Europe Agreement on the Transfer of Corpses gives repatriation a clear legal framework, something not every European destination offers. That framework does not remove Bulgaria's own domestic requirements, though. One of them, mandatory embalming, shapes every case, whatever the cause of death.

## Embalming is not a choice

Every international repatriation from Bulgaria requires embalming by a licensed Bulgarian funeral director. It is not offered as an option to reduce cost, and it is not skipped for a straightforward natural death. The embalming certificate feeds directly into the laissez-passer application, the transit permit issued by the Bulgarian Ministry of Foreign Affairs, so the two steps are tied together.

## A sudden death brings in the Prosecutor

Where a death is sudden, unexplained, or the cause is unclear, the Regional Prosecutor's Office authorises release of the body to the forensic institute. This adds three to seven days on its own. Families sometimes ask whether a private arrangement can move things faster. It cannot. The Prosecutor's involvement is a legal requirement, not an administrative formality that can be negotiated.

## Resort deaths add a transfer step

Sunny Beach, Golden Sands, and Albena draw large numbers of British holidaymakers every summer, and deaths at these resorts are common enough that this site sees the pattern repeat year after year. All three sit some distance from Sofia. That means an internal transfer before the paperwork can begin in earnest. Regional administrative offices outside the capital generally process documentation more slowly than Sofia does, and the gap widens during the July to August peak.

## The flight home is the easier part

Once the Bulgarian documentation is complete, the international leg is comparatively simple. Sofia Airport has direct routes to London Heathrow and Gatwick with several carriers, so there is no need to route through a third country. Human remains travel in a hermetically sealed, zinc-lined coffin under the Council of Europe Agreement, a standard specification any UK funeral director will recognise on arrival.
