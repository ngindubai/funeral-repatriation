Bringing a loved one home from Angola is more involved than most families expect walking in, mainly because of two things: the paperwork is entirely in Portuguese, and funeral infrastructure outside Luanda is thin on the ground. If your relative worked in the oil sector, there's a third factor that usually helps rather than hinders: an employer already used to this kind of situation.

## What starts the process

For a sudden, unexplained, or violent death, the Servico de Investigacao Criminal, the SIC, must clear the body for release before any embalming or preparation can begin. A natural death in hospital moves faster: the attending doctor issues the Certidao de Obito, the civil registry records it, and the British Embassy in Luanda, which is resident and provides consular registration directly, can register the death without the additional SIC step.

## The oil industry difference

Angola has a significant British expatriate presence in oil and gas, with companies including BP and TotalEnergies operating in and around Luanda and offshore in the Kwanza Basin. When an employee dies, HR teams at these companies typically move quickly: activating corporate travel insurance or group life cover, some of which includes repatriation directly. Employer HR sometimes holds power of attorney on the family's behalf, meaning the family may never need to handle Angolan paperwork personally. It's worth asking directly what the employer's duty-of-care protocol actually covers before engaging separate support.

Offshore deaths add a layer: coordination with INAMAR, Angola's maritime authority, and sometimes a recovery vessel before any aviation transfer to the mainland can happen at all. Employer logistics teams are usually already managing this by the time the family in the UK is informed.

## Why the paperwork itself takes time

Every document is in Portuguese. UK authorities require certified English translations before anything can be used for import, and a sworn translator's involvement adds days that can't really be shortened. Outside Luanda, funeral infrastructure is limited, so a death in a remote province can mean transporting the body to the capital before preparation even begins.
