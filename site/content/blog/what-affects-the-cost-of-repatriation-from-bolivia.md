Two things make Bolivia a genuinely distinctive case among South American repatriations: the country is landlocked, and its most popular tourist altitude can turn an apparently simple death into a legal one.

## Altitude sickness is treated as unnatural

Soroche, altitude sickness, kills a small but real number of UK visitors to La Paz, Lake Titicaca, the Uyuni salt flat, and the wider Altiplano each year, usually through sudden pulmonary or cerebral oedema. Whatever the medical picture looks like, Bolivian law classes these as unnatural deaths. That brings in the FELCC, Bolivia's specialist crime unit, and the Fiscalia, the prosecutor's office, and a post-mortem at the Instituto de Investigaciones Forenses is typically ordered as a result. Don't assume a soroche death will move quickly just because the cause seems obvious; the legal process runs regardless.

## Landlocked, and high, at the same time

Bolivia has no direct flights to the UK, so every case routes through a connecting hub. La Paz's El Alto Airport sits at 4,061 metres, the highest commercial airport in the world, and that altitude genuinely limits how much cargo an aircraft can carry. Most repatriations route instead through the lower Santa Cruz Viru Viru Airport, connecting via Lima or Sao Paulo before reaching Heathrow.

## Remote locations add a transfer stage first

A death at the Salar de Uyuni salt flat or the Tiwanaku archaeological site needs to reach La Paz before any official process can start. A death in the Amazon basin around Rurrenabaque needs road or light aircraft evacuation first. Neither the Fiscalia process nor the documentation chain can begin until the deceased has reached somewhere with the right facilities.

## Spanish documentation, throughout

The Acta de Defuncion from SERECI, the Fiscalia release order, the IDIF post-mortem report, and the export permit are all produced in Spanish. Certified English translation is a standard, unavoidable step for every one of them.
