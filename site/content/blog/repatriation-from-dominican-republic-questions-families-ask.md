Most British visitors to the Dominican Republic never leave the Punta Cana resort corridor, and that single fact shapes almost everything about how a repatriation from here plays out. From Cap Cana down to Uvero Alto, this stretch of coast sees the overwhelming majority of British deaths in the country too.

That concentration cuts both ways. Local funeral directors here are genuinely experienced with foreign national cases, thanks to the sheer volume of American and European tourism. But two structural facts still shape every case: there's no resident British Embassy, and INACIF, the national forensic institute, gets involved more often than families expect.

## No embassy on the ground

The United Kingdom doesn't maintain a resident Embassy in the Dominican Republic. Consular responsibility sits with the British Embassy in Port of Spain, Trinidad, or, depending on the arrangement, the British High Commission in Kingston, Jamaica. Both cover the country remotely.

What that means in practice: don't expect a consular officer to appear at the resort on the day of the death. The FCDO Emergency Travel Line, 020 7008 5000, is the right first call, and it coordinates with whichever office is handling the case. In the meantime, the resort's own emergency contact and the local funeral director are usually the first practical points of contact, not British officials.

## INACIF and why it gets involved

INACIF, the Instituto Nacional de Ciencias Forenses, has jurisdiction whenever a death is sudden, unexplained, or carries any suggestion of criminal involvement. At resorts this often means drowning, cardiac events, or anything without an immediately obvious cause. Once INACIF is involved, the body can't move until its investigation concludes. Straightforward cases clear in about a week. More complex ones, or those caught during a period of high caseload, take longer.

## What the documents look like

Everything is issued in Spanish and needs certified English translation before it's usable in the UK. Punta Cana International Airport handles both passenger and cargo traffic and is the departure point for most cases.

## The route home

No direct flights connect the Dominican Republic to the UK. Cargo routes via the United States, most often Miami, New York JFK, or Charlotte, then on to Heathrow with a transatlantic carrier.
